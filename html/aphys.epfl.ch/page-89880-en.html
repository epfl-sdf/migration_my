Particle distribution in Lakes

 

There are two types of particles in lake water, terrigenous- and biological particles. Terrigenous particles are mainly supplied to lakes by rivers. This supply can be substantial during spring melting and major precipitation events. Organic particle production can be extensive during spring and summer algae blooms and are sometimes amplified by eutrophication. The particles will eventually end up on the lake bottom as sediment. The time it takes for the particles to sediment to the bottom are determined by the size, weight and shape of the particles. Turbulence in the water can counteract the downward particle movement and even resuspend sedimented particles. Currents and waves can therefore transport particles a considerable distance before sedimentation occurs.

Lake based water supply systems depend on intake of water with low particle content to minimize the amount of purification that is needed. This became apparent during the winter of 2009/2010 when extremely high particle content in Lake Biel (Bielersee), see Fig. 1, forced the temporary closure of the Ipsach/Bienne water treatment plant in the city of Biel, Switzerland. The aim of this project is hence to develop an experimental procedure to minimize particle intake into the water works. The project will focus on answering the following questions:

 

·         What were the causes of the turbidity event, leading to a closure of the water treatment plant in Biel during early January 2010? Under which circumstances could this be repeated?

·         What quantities of particles are washed into the Lake of Biel and where are they deposited?

·         What are the predominant flow patterns?

·         What are the currents in the Lake and how does these relate to (a) particle entry and particle deposition, (b) the flow pattern predicted by model calculations and (c) to the temperature changes in the lake and its tributaries (Aare, Suze and Zhilkanal)?

·         Which place in the River Suze would be the ideal sampling point with respect particle content and water quality?

·         Which areas in Lake Biel whould qualify for the placement of a new water intake given reasonable distances to the water treatment plant during: (i) normal external/internal Lake conditions, (ii) flood events, (iii) changing temperatures in the lake and/or tributaries, (iv) changing precipitation ratios summer/winter, (v) the effects of water power plants in the tributaries and (vi) the effect of the nuclear power plant Muhlenberg?

 

APHYS has started with a 2 year field measurement campaign in Lake Biel in order to understand the Lake. We were fortunate to have our sensors out during the storm events that hit the western part of Switzerland during the middle of June this year, the maximum measured wind velocity was 33 m/s. The temperature data clearly shows the arrival of the storms as a massive increase of surface water mixing and generation of up to 10 meter high internal waves in the thermocline, see Fig. 2.
