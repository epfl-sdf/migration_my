Sensorimotor control of fine grasping in non-human primates

Positions filled, new projects coming up in December 2018.

Introduction

Information about body position and movement (proprioception) is critical to the central nervous system in order to adapt the generation of movement to its environment. We use our limbs to manipulate objects, but also to communicate with and understand our environment. During these interactions, we use among others touch and proprioception as feedback to adjust our movements and actions with the aim of fulfilling a task, such as opening a door or carrying a bag of groceries. However, while touch as feedback modality has been extensively studied, proprioception and its importance for movement control and postural adjustment are far less understood

Representation of reaching & grasping different objects in S1 area 2

Project Description: We aim at gaining new insights into the role of proprioception during skilled motor tasks in non-human primates and aim to understand how behavioral output (arm and hand kinematics, grasp force) is related to cortical representations of movement (M1) and proprioception (S1 area 2) while grasping different types of objects. For this, we will record a NHP during a reach and grasp task with different objects to grasp.

Data & analysis: From behavioral experiments - S1 and M1 intracortical recording, 3D joint kinematics of arm, hand and fingers, grasping force through pressure sensor on object, pulling force through impedance measurement of robotic arm presenting the object. Analysis will consist of spike sorting/multiunit activity of S1 to relate specific contacts to the different phases of movement, correlation S1-M1 during the phases of the movement 

Requirements: background in signal processing, Matlab or R for data analysis

Start: June 2018

Contact: sophie.wurth@epfl.ch

Representation of peripheral nerve stimulation (PNS) in S1 area 2

Project Description: The mechanisms of signaling hand proprioception via the peripheral nerves and how these signals are encoded in the primary somatosensory cortex remain elusive. In this framework, we will compare the responses obtained in S1 by tendon vibration or during passive finger and hand joint movements with selective intraneural subthreshold PNS to recruit large myelinated afferent fibers (Ia fibers mediating proprioception) within the median and radial nerves. We will analyze how the natural (through passive movement or tendon vibration) and artificial (through PNS) fiber recruitment is represented in the primary somatosensory cortex representing the hand and forearm. We will further assess whether the elicited activity can be modulated (amplitude, pulse width, frequency) and we will determine the stability of these responses over time. The data collection will be carried out during anesthetized experiments in non-human primates (NHP) implanted with intracortical arrays in S1 and M1, intraneural electrodes in the median and ulnar nerves, and intramuscular EMG wire electrodes in hand and forearm muscles.

Data & analysis: From sensory (S1) recruitment curve experiments under anesthesia: hand/forearm S1 area 2 intracortical recordings, hand M1 intracortical recordings, EMG signals from hand and forearm muscles, ENG signals from peripheral nerve implants during passive joint movement or tendon vibration trials

Requirements: background in signal processing, Matlab or R for data analysis

Start: September 2018

Contact: sophie.wurth@epfl.ch

Proprioceptive feedback mediated by peripheral nerve stimulation during behavioral task

Project Description: The goal of this project is to understand whether we are able to elicit proprioceptive signals by subthreshold peripheral nerve stimulation (PNS) during behavioral experiments in non-human primates. For this, we will trigger PNS during the reaching phase of the movement and record arm and hand EMG signals and joint kinematics to see whether the stimulation modifies the movement, and if so, how this change is represented in S1 compared to unperturbed trials. 

Data & analysis: From behavioral experiments - hand/forearm S1 area 2 intraneural recordings, hand M1 recordings, EMG signals from hand and forearm muscles, hand and arm 3D joint kinematics, force signals from grasping and pulling

Requirements: background in signal processing, comfortable in Matlab or R

Start: September 2018

Contact: sophie.wurth@epfl.ch

Grasping movement prediction based on primary motor cortex recordings

Project Description: This project consists in analysing intracortical brain signals from of the primary motor cortex of a monkey in order to decode the different phases of grasping movement. The animal will be implanted with a 64 channels microarray in the hand area of M1 and will be performing a reach and grasp task where he has to pull on an object of a certain shape. The kinematics of the arm and hand will be recorded using motion capture system.
The first step will consist in implementing an offline LDA decoder based on kinematics to predict the phase of the grasping movement (i.e 1) reaching and extension of the wrist, 2) shaping of the hand 3) grasping and pulling.
In a second step, this decoder will then be implemented online to predict in real time the kinematics of the hand and to compare it to the recorded kinematics during the movement.

Data & analysis: From behavioral experiments – hand M1 area area, hand and arm 3D joint kinematics.

Suitable for: master project, master students internships, condensed semester project or lab immersion (for instance 5 weeks in summer) – better full time

Requirements: background in machine learning and signal processing, comfortable in Matlab / C++

Start: From June 2018 onward

Contact: marion.badi-dubois@epfl.ch

Kinematics tracking of fine grasping movement and force analysis

Project Description: The goal of this project consists in analysis kinematic (movement in 3D), and kinetic (force) components during fine grasping movement. The data will be extracted from a monkey performing a reach-and-grasp task where a robotic arm presents a target object to the animal, which has to reach it, grasp it, pull it, and then release it. The kinematics of the arm and hand as well as the grasping force applied to the objects and the pulling force exerted by the animal will be recorded in real time. The project will consist in extracting the 3D trajectories, joint position/speed/acceleration, and in analyzing the grasping and pulling forces. Those data will be compared for objects of different shapes and size and for various level of resistance applied during the pulling movement.

Data & analysis: From behavioral experiments –hand and arm 3D joint kinematics, force signals from grasping and pulling.

Suitable for: bachelor project, master project, students internships, condensed semester project or lab immersion (for instance 5 weeks in summer) – better full time

Requirements: comfortable in Matlab

Start: From April 2018 onward

Contact: marion.badi-dubois@epfl.ch

Computational modeling of peripheral nerve stimulation in the primate arm nerves

Project Description: This project consists in completing a computational model of the primate median, ulnar and radial nerves in order to predict the recruitment of motor fibers during intraneural electrical stimulation.
Based on a simplified 3D representation of the arm nerve segment, the student will have to model clusters of fibers within the 3D volume, to compute the degree of activation of those fibers under different stimulation settings (e.g, location and orientation of the implant, different current amplitude and frequency of stimulation) and to compare this activation with the one obtained during in-vivo experiments.

Data & analysis: From histological samples, based on the literature on computational modeling, and from behavioral experiments.

Suitable for: master project, master students internships – better full time

Requirements: background in matlab and python

Start: From April 2018

Contact: marion.badi-dubois@epfl.ch

Activation of grasp function by intraneural stimulation: data processing and muscular signal analysis

Project description: This project consists in analyzing muscular signals recorded from muscles that control fingers and the wrist movements (recruitment curves, selectivity computation…). The main goal is to determine the selectivity achieved using TIME stimulation in the median, ulnar and radial nerves. It will allow to determine the order of recruitment of muscles and to derive stimulation strategies for the restoration of different types of grasp.

Requirements: good programming skills in Matlab.

Best for: semester or master project

Remark: The study takes place at the Swiss Neuro-Translational Primate Platform of Fribourg, Switzerland. Some of the projects presented below will be conducted at the TNE lab in Geneva and other will be conducted in Fribourg.

​​​​​Contact: marion.badi-dubois@epfl.ch

Design of an experimental setup for grasping force measurement

Project description: this project is divided into two parts.

Part 1: Advanced robotic for animal machine interaction

In order to measure grasping force performed by the animal, the experimental setup is equipped with a robotic arm linked to 3D flexible grasping targets measuring the pressure exerted by the hand. The aim of this project is to build a controllable interface between the robot and the sensor and to calibrate the sensitive objects. The student should also implement a system of visual cues (lights) that will be indicated to the animal its performance in the task.

Part 2: Exploratory project – design of a fine force control task

The goal of this project is to implement an experimental paradigm for fine force control in a monkey. The student will have to design a behavioural paradigm in which the animal has to control in a precise manner the force exerted on a sensitive object while providing a visual feedback.

Requirements: this project will include some hardware (electronics…) and some programming. Students should feel comfortable writing code (C/C++, Matlab).

Best for: semester or master project

Remark: Part 1 and 2 can be performed as two separated projects.

The study takes place at the Swiss Neuro-Translational Primate Platform of Fribourg, Switzerland. Some of the projects presented below will be conducted at the TNE lab in Geneva and other will be conducted in Fribourg.
