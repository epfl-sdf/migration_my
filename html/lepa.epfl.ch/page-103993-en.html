    About the project

    The dual circuit redox flow battery

    Figure 1: Schematic of the dual-circuit redox flow battery for indirect water electrolysis

    Project Summary

    The dual-circuit RFB

    The dual-circuit system combines a conventional redox flow battery (RFB) with two catalytic reactors in separate external circuits. The RFB is conventionally charged from the grid or with renewable energy but may then be discharged by one of two ways: conventional discharge through a load or, the chemical discharge of each electrolyte via catalytic beds to generate hydrogen on the negative side and an oxidation on the positive side. These reactions regenerate the discharged redox mediators, which are then redirected back into the RFB to repeat the charging process. The system therefore combines the advantages of a classical RFB with a chemical route to hydrogen production on demand. This approach is designed to complement electrochemical energy storage and may circumvent the low energy density of RFBs especially as hydrogen can be produced continuously whilst the RFB is charging. The concept is shown in Figure 1 for a vanadium-cerium RFB.

     

    The demonstrator

    In November 2013 LEPA received funding from the SFOE to collaborate with Sinergy, the CREM and the district of Martigny to install and adapt  a 10 kW electrochemical storage system in Martigny, Valais. The objective is to install the commercial RFB into the isolated electricity network in Martigny, and -

    1) Study its impact, as bought, on the local energy network.

    2) Install the novel, Swiss patented, secondary circuit and evaluate its effectiveness.

    RFB technology is growing globally and installations are present all over the world. Switzerland has yet to test a large RFB in the field however, therefore this will be the first study of RFB renewable energy storage in the Swiss network to-date. The objective is to install the battery and use as intended for at least 3 months to ascertain its benefits and shortcomings. In the summer of 2014 we will adapt the RFB to the dual-circuit arrangement. The objective is to be generating batches of renewable hydrogen come August 2014.

    This project will be part of a larger endeavour aimed at creating a large-scale systemic demonstrator in Valais/Wallis as part of a common effort by the Canton, EPFL, HES-SO Valais and all interested local stakeholders.

    Indirect water electrolyis

    Conventional water electrolysis

    Hydrogen is being studied extensively as a means of storing renewable energy and providing carbon free energy via fuel cells. Theoretically hydrogen is an excellent energy carrier, but to reap its benefits and transition smoothly into a functioning hydrogen economy, it must be produced, distributed and consumed efficiently and at low cost using clean and sustainable means. Unfortunately hydrogen is predominantly produced by the classical reforming of natural gas or coal, an energy intensive and polluting process. Converting renewable power to hydrogen gas using centralised large-scale electrolysers is growing however. Two major types of electrolyser are the alkaline and the proton-exchange-membrane (PEM) electrolysers.

    Water electrolysis is the process of using electricity to split water into its composite elements, hydrogen and oxygen. A voltage is applied across two electrodes such that protons are reduced to hydrogen gas at the negative electrode and  water is oxidised to oxygen at the positive electrode. The electrolysis of water is the oldest electrochemical reaction known, and has been studied for the "clean" production of hydrogen as a fuel for over a century.

    Thermodynamically the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) should proceed at 0 V vs. the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) and the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) at 1.23 V vs. SHE (assuming the lower heating value (LHV) for hydrogen) at pH 0.

    2H+ + 2e-  → H2           E0 = 0 V 

    H2O → ½O2 + 4e- + 4H+       E0 = 1.23 V

    Both reactions involve multiple proton and electron steps however, hydrogen requiring 2 electrons 2 protons, and oxygen 4 electron and 4 protons. This imparts a kinetic limitation on both reaction, especially the OER. This means that more energy is required to force the reactions to happen. In electrochemistry this extra energy is called the overpotential. Using more energy makes the production of hydrogen less energy efficient, so a major research area lies in trying to decrease this overpotential using catalysts. The most common catalyst used for both water oxidation and reduction is platinum, which can reduce the overpotential for hydrogen to ca. 100 mV and water oxidation to 300 mV at its best.

    Conventional electrolysers generally operate at around 2 V, an overall 770 mV overpotential. Energy efficiency is lost to complications such as gas bubbles at the electrode increasing the cell resistance, corrosion of the electrode and catalyst poisoning. The cost of precious metal catalysts in PEM electrolysers also makes the capital cost very high and consequently the cost of hydrogen.

     

    Indirect water electrolysis

    This dual-circuit RFB aims to achieve electrochemical water splitting indirectly.  This approach separates the electrolytic step (electrode-solution charge transfer) from the chemical reaction of interest (oxidation or reduction of water) and is therefore governed by different kinetic requirements and reaction limitations. For water electrolysis, such a process would avoid;

    1. Expensive, conductive catalysts
    2. Electrode fouling
    3. Gas production damage at the electrode
    4. Large overpotentials to drive faster reactions

    This approach essentially aims to mediate proton reduction and water oxidation via the redox species in the RFB. Lets first consider hydrogen evolution: The negative electrolyte (V2+/3+) in a conventional all-vanadium redox flow battery has standard potential of  E0 = -0.26 V.  Thermodynamically this means there is enough driving force in the negative electrolyte to drive the reduction of protons to hydrogen (E0 = 0 V), given the appropriate catalyst, with a 260 mV overpotential. The kinetic requirements to produce the V2+ are considerably less than for hydrogen evolution on a carbon electrode however, therefore the V2+ can be generated with a comparable efficiency to direct water reduction at an electrocatalytic surface.

    2V2+ + 2H+ → 2V3+ + ↑H2        

    On the positive V4+/5+ side of a VRB however the redox potential is 1 V presenting a shortfall of 230 mV on the 1.23 V necessary to thermodynamically oxidise water. V(V) cannot oxidise water to oxygen and protons. The cerium redox couple has a potential of 1.44 V is sulfuric acid. The Ce4+ species therefore has 210 mV of oxidative power to drive water oxidation.

    4Ce4+ + 2H2O → 4Ce3+ + 4H+ + ↑O2

    This interplay of thermodynamic potentials is illustrated in the following figure. The blue lines are water splitting reactions to hydrogen and oxygen.

    Catalysing the reactions 

    Both hydrogen evolution and water oxidation are kinetically limited reactions and despite suitable electrochemical potentials to drive the reactions, catalysts are still required. In an indirect approach the catalysts have far fewer restrictions than those deposited on an electrode surface. the main advantages in the catalyst materials are:

    1. Not restricted to conductive substrates
    2. Catalyst may be deposited onto any substrate
    3. Considerably lower cost materials may be used
    4. The catalyst architecture has considerably greater versatility

    To achieve indirect water splitting using RFB electrolytes we use the V(II) and Ce(IV) species. The main focus of our research has been to use the negative electrolyte to produce hydrogen and capture "junk" electricity. Our hydrogen evolution catalyst is very low cost and versatile molybdenum carbide (Mo2C) as opposed to platinum and platinum group metals conventionally used in hydrogen evolution.

    Water oxidation is a little bit more difficult than proton reduction, due to the stability of the water molecule. For this reaction we are still limited to platinum group oxides to catalyse the reaction, presently. In sulfuric acid with Ce(IV) the redox potential is 1.44 V vs. SHE. The most effective catalyst we identified for these conditions is partially hydrated RuO2.

    Below are two photographs of our "catalytic beds" directly converting the charged electrolytes to hydrogen (left) and oxygen (right) whilst regenerating the discharged redox species. Videos of the reactions can be found in the Pictures and Videos section.

    Adapting the all-vanadium system

    The hydrogen reactor

    The engineering of this reactor is unique, as it operates in batch mode with respect to the RFB i.e. we extract a fixed volume of V(II) (20L) and control the reaction through the H2 reactor. However, the reaction itself is continuous flow where a liquid phase reacts on a solid electrolyte to produce a converted liquid phase and gaseous product. To our knowledge conventional chemical engineering has not considered such a reaction process before, at least not thoroughly. The hydrogen reactor for this demonstrator plant has been designed to consider a number of crucial issues:

    1.  Safe operation at pressures between 2-10 bar
    2.  Effective separation of hydrogen and liquid
    3.  Thorough filtration step to remove all catalysts from V(III)
    4.  Continuous flow of electrolyte over catalyst

    Consequently we have decided on a horizontal catalytic bed design. The tubes are divided by baffles to encourage the flow of electrolyte underneath then through the catalytic bed, preventing dead space. The head-space above collects the hydrogen across the whole tube, and this is removed using a special compressed air pump. The discharged liquid phase is diverted through both catalytic tubes to ensure conversion is complete, and then is passed through two fine mesh filtration systems. Analysis of the state-of-charge of the discharged electrolyte will then be made prior to returning the electrolyte to the battery. This is all depicted in the following scheme.

     

    Catalysing all-vanadium reactions

    In the previous discussion our initial studies used a vanadium-cerium RFB to achieve total water splitting (i.e. 2H2O → 2H2 + O2). Commercial RFBs are not V-Ce however, but all-vanadium. The commercial 10 kWh system we are purchasing will have an all vanadium electrolyte, and this complicated the water oxidation process. Referring back to Figure 4 we see that V(IV) has a redox potential more negative than water oxidation. This means it is not oxidising enough to permit the water oxidation reaction.

    Cross-contamination of different metal ions through the membrane in RFBS means that mixed-ion systems are rarely used on the prolonged, large scale. Advances in membrane technology may later permit the use of mixed metal ions, but presently the technology is limited to all-vanadium electrolytes. In order to allow for chemical discharge of V(II) to hydrogen and V(III) we must also develop a means of discharging V(V) to V(IV) and produce protons (H+) to balance the electrical charges and loss of protons to hydrogen.

    Although unable to oxidise water, V(V) is a strong oxidising agent, and may be used to oxidise a variety of species. Indeed the counter reaction may be used in a useful process such as waste water treatment or the destruction of pollutants. We have a number of reactions presently under investigation to chemically discharge the positive electrolyte.

    1. Oxidation of hydrazine (N2H4 – rocket fuel)
    2. Oxidation of sulfur dioxide (SO2) to sulfuric acid
    3. Oxidation of waste water organic materials (e.g. phenols, catechol)

     

    Hydrazine

    Vanadium (V) reacts rapidly with hydrazine to evolve benign N2 and produce protons

     N2H4 + 4VO2+ + 4H+ → ↑N2 + 4VO2+ + 4H2O

     

    Sulfur Dioxide

    Vanadium (V) also reacts rapidly with SO2, a well-known pollutant produced in coal power plants, to produce sulfuric acid.

    SO2 + 2VO2+ + 4H+ → H2SO4 + 2VO2+ + 2H+

    This reaction is of particular interest to our project. The redox potential for SO2 oxidation is somewhere between -0.1 – 0.3 V Therefore it is capable of reducing V(V) and V(IV). SO2 is also very soluble in water and saturated solution called sulfurous acid can reach concentrations akin to those of the V(V) in the RFB (i.e. ca. 1.5 M). Finally, this reaction produces a useful commodity following oxidation, unlike the evolution of oxygen or nitrogen from the aforementioned processes.

    Preliminary results using sulfurous acid and V(V) have shown a rapid reaction reducing the V(V) to V(IV) when used in stoichiometric amounts. Of course, this would lead to a build up of H2SO4 concentration in the electrolyte, therefore we cannot use it directly just yet.
