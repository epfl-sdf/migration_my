Saturday guest speakers and abstracts

March 15, 2014

Nancy Y. Ip, PhD

Dean of Science
The Morningside Professor of Life Science
Chair Professor, Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://life-sci.ust.hk/faculty/Prof.N.Ip/index.html

From Understanding Neural Plasticity to Drug Discovery for Neurodegenerative Diseases
Neurodegenerative diseases, such as Alzheimer’s disease, are emerging to become a major health burden in societies with a large aging population. Increasing evidence suggests that dysfunction in neural communication plays a key role in the onset and progression of neurodegenerative diseases. Neurons in the brain communicate with each other through chemical transmission at specialized structures known as synapses. Experience-dependent modification of synaptic transmission or synaptic plasticity forms the cellular basis of cognitive functions such as learning and memory. Loss of synapses or impaired synaptic plasticity has been observed at early stages of Alzheimer’s disease before extensive neuronal death. Thus, explicating the molecular mechanisms involved in the establishment and remodeling of synapses is pivotal for identifying new drug targets and developing neurotherapeutic agents.

Synaptic remodeling is believed to be precisely regulated by the co-ordinated activation of diverse cell surface receptors. My laboratory is interested in understanding how neurons transduce signals from these cell surface receptors to regulate synapse function and plasticity of the adult brain under normal and diseased conditions. We have recently identified distinct receptor-mediated signaling pathways that can positively or negatively regulate neurotransmission, thereby differentially promote the strengthening and weakening of existing synapses. For example, the receptor tyrosine kinase TrkB binds to the growth factor BDNF and modulates structural changes of the synapse during memory formation. Similarly, the G-protein-coupled receptor melanocortin-4 receptor (MC4R), the cognate receptor of the neuropeptide α-MSH, enhances hippocampal synaptic plasticity through the regulation of synapse morphology and the trafficking of neurotransmitter receptors. On the other hand, another receptor tyrosine kinase, EphA4, is a major negative regulator of neurotransmission. Activation of EphA4 attenuates synaptic transmission by promoting the degradation of neurotransmitter receptors and the loss of synaptic contacts. We further demonstrate that EphA4 is important in mediating the synaptic dysfunctions during the progression of Alzheimer’s disease, suggesting that this receptor is a promising target for developing new treatments for this devastating neurodegenerative disease.

In our attempt to translate our discoveries from basic research to the development of neurotherapeutic agents, we have leveraged our research strengths in molecular neuroscience and Chinese medicine (CM) to establish a focused drug discovery program in search of new drug leads, including those that can regulate synaptic activity. Our major objective is to utilize a systematic knowledge-based approach to screen for neuroactive lead compounds from CM. Through discovery of new molecular targets in neurodegenerative diseases, we have screened for potential modulators of these targets using specific bioassay platforms or structure-based molecular docking approach. Natural compounds from CM that display high binding affinity to specific molecular targets have been identified and characterized. This drug discovery strategy has proven to be a very effective approach in identifying potential drug candidates, some of which are currently at various stages of pre-clinical development for neurological disorders.

Biography
Nancy Ip is currently the Dean of Science, The Morningside Professor of Life Science, and Director of the State Key Laboratory of Molecular Neuroscience at The Hong Kong University of Science and Technology (HKUST). She received her PhD degree in Pharmacology from Harvard Medical School, and was Senior Staff Scientist at Regeneron Pharmaceuticals Inc., New York. Since joining HKUST in 1993, she has served as Associate Dean of Science (1998-2005), Director of the Biotechnology Research Institute (1996-2008), and Head of the Department of Biochemistry (2000-2009).

Nancy Ip is well-known for her seminal discoveries in the biology of neurotrophic factors, which are proteins that promote the survival, development and maintenance of neurons in the nervous system. She has made important contributions towards understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying brain development and synaptic plasticity, and their dysregulation in neurological disorders. She also plays a significant role in the development of biotechnology in Hong Kong. She has helped to establish the expertise and capabilities to drive local drug discovery efforts, and launched prominent collaborations with major biopharmaceutical companies, thus placing HKUST on the map for cutting-edge R&D in molecular neuroscience.

As a highly accomplished researcher, Nancy Ip has published over 230 scientific papers with ~ 16,000 SCI citations, and holds 23 patents. She is also the senior editor of the Journal of Neuroscience, and an elected Councillor for two leading organizations in the fields of neuroscience and psychopharmacology: the Society for Neuroscience and the CINP (Collegium Internationale Neuro-Psychopharmacologicum). In recognition of her excellent achievements in science and biotechnology, Nancy Ip has received numerous awards and honors. These include the Croucher Foundation Senior Research Fellowship; the National Natural Science Awards, China’s highest honor in the natural sciences; the L’OREAL-UNESCO for Women in Science Award, making her the first honoree in the life sciences from China; the Scientific and Technological Progress Prize of Ho Leung Ho Lee Foundation; and the Chevalier de l’Ordre National du Mérite. As a further testament to her professional achievement, she was elected Academician of the Chinese Academy of Sciences and Fellow of the Academy of Sciences for the Developing World.

Patrick Fraering, PhD

Assistant Professor
Brain Mind Institute
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://fraering-lab.epfl.ch/

Notch-sparing modulation of Gamma-secretase for the safe treatment of Alzheimer's disease
The amyloid-b (Ab) senile plaques, one of the principal histopathological hallmarks of Alzheimer’s disease (AD), are mainly constituted of the highly hydrophobic Ab42 peptides prone to aggregation and fibrillarization. As γ-secretase is directly implicated in the generation of these toxic Aβ peptides, inhibiting the activity of this enzyme constitutes an attractive therapeutic strategy to prevent or slow down the disease progression. However, recent phase 3 clinical trials testing γ-secretase inhibitors (GSIs) revealed severe side effects attributed to impaired Notch receptor signaling, critically involved in cell fate regulation. To selectively reduce Abproduction without affecting other γ-secretase functions, we developed a new therapeutic approach to selectively neutralize the Aβ Precursor Protein C-terminal substrate (APP-C99) with monoclonal antibodies. Our selected antibodies bound accessible N- or C-terminal epitopes of this substrate and led to reduced Aβ levels in vitro and in vivo. Selective lowering of Ab42 with Notch-sparing small molecule g-secretase modulators (GSMs) is another promising therapeutic approach for AD. We recently demonstrated that second generation clinical GSMs effectively shift production of toxic Ab42 towards the shorter Ab38, but unequivocally spare the e-cleavage sites of both APP and Notch substrates. In addition, we show that GSMs conserve their Ab42-lowering and Ab38-raising properties on different substrates with mutations causing familial AD. GSMs can thus be used for the treatment of patients with both sporadic and genetic forms of AD.

Biography
P. Fraering studied biology at the University Louis Pasteur of Strasbourg (France), where he earned a master’s degree in biochemistry (1995) and pre-doctoral research degree in molecular and cellular biology at the CNRS (1996). In 2001, he received his PhD, conducting functional studies of the GPI-transamidase complex and protein secretion at the University of Fribourg, Switzerland. In 2002, he had a joint appointment in the labs of Prof. D. Selkoe and Dr. M. Wolfe at Harvard Medical School where he studied Alzheimer’s disease and the structure and function of γ-secretase, the protease responsible for the production of the Alzheimer’s disease amyloid-b peptides. In 2007, he has been appointed assistant professor at the EPFL’s School of Life Sciences.

Bernard Schneider, PhD

Research Associate
Brain Mind Institute
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://len.epfl.ch/

Gene-based therapeutic approaches for neurodegenerative diseases
Gene-based therapies offer novel opportunities for the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases affecting the central nervous system. In pathologies where the genetic causes have been identified, gene therapy is a rational approach to correct deficiencies in the affected cells, and thereby change the course of the disease. However, it is critical to establish vector systems and routes of administration that can deliver therapeutic molecules to cells that are distributed over large structures such as the brain and spinal cord.
Our laboratory explores novel technologies, including viral vectors and encapsulated cell transplants, to develop gene-based therapeutics against motor neuron and Alzheimer’s diseases. We will present experimental evidence for the therapeutic efficacy of adeno-associated viral vectors to suppress the expression of mutated SOD1 in the spinal cord of a mouse model of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS). In particular, we are investigating in which cell type(s) SOD1 expression has to be targeted to correct the cell autonomous and non-cell autonomous pathogenic processes involved in ALS.

In the context of Alzheimer’s disease, we are developing immunotherapy based on genetically modified cells secreting recombinant antibodies directed against amyloid beta. For implantation in allogeneic recipients, the antibody-secreting cells are encapsulated in a device composed of polymer permeable membranes. We will present data showing that this approach can be used for passive immunization to clear the amyloid deposits that accumulate in animal models of Alzheimer’s disease.

Overall, we propose that novel tools for gene therapy will broaden the arsenal of disease-modifying treatments against neurodegenerative diseases.

Biography
Bernard Schneider obtained a MS in Biology at the University of Fribourg in Switzerland and completed his PhD in Medical Biology at the University of Lausanne in 2001, followed by a postdoctoral work at the University of Wisconsin in Madison, USA. Dr Schneider joined the EPFL in 2006, and is now senior scientist and project leader at the Brain Mind Institute in the EPFL Life Science Faculty. His research interests are mainly focused on the study of neurodegenerative diseases including Parkinson’s disease and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), in particular towards the development of ex vivo and in vivo gene therapy approaches.

Karl Herrup, PhD

Head and Chair Professor
Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://herruplab.ust.hk/

Cell Cycle Control in the Adult Neuron – and the Penalty of Failure
The adult neurons of the central nervous system (CNS) have an uneasy relationship with the cell cycle. The last cell division of most neurons occurs sometime during embryogenesis or during the first year of life. This situation led to the description of adult CNS neurons as 'permanently postmitotic'. There are now multiple lines of evidence for an alternative viewpoint, however: neurons are aggressively postmitotic, but there are fully capable of initiating a cell cycle and proceeding most or all of the way through S-phase complete with DNA replication. Unfortunately, rather than finish the cycle, the hyperploid neurons stall before M-phase and die. This cycle-induced death is observed in many situations involving nerve cell death, including neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, ALS and others. The question arises, therefore, if a neuron can enter a cycle but does not, then there must be factors that are resident in healthy neurons that prevent this. We have discovered several of these factors, including the PI3kinase, ATM. Surprisingly, while ATM is well known as a cell cycle checkpoint protein, it exerts many of its neuroprotective (anti-mitotic) actions by means of the epigenome. ATM controls the level and cellular localization of the class IIa histone deacetylase, HDAC4; it also regulates the levels of the polycomb group protein, EZH2. In the absence of ATM activity, the histones are less acetylated and more methylated. This leads to a more condensed chromatin with the expected reduction in the transcription of neuronal survival genes. This engagement of the epigenome means that the ATM phenotype can be modified by environmental changes that also induce epigenetic changes. One example of this is the simple amino acid, glutamine. By altering the response levels of many different stress-response proteins, increased glutamine concentration can significantly affect the response of the cell to different insults.

The findings emphasize the importance of strict cell cycle control, not just to prevent tumors, but to avoid neurodegenerative illness.  And the involvement of epigenetics has important clinical implications as environmental manipulations (e.g., the levels of glutamine) can dramatically change the outcome of neurodegenerative disease.

Biography
Dr. Karl Herrup received his Bachelor’s degree from Brandeis University in Waltham, MA and his Ph.D. in Neuroscience from Stanford University in 1974. After two postdoctoral fellowships – in Neurogenetics at Children’s Hospital/Harvard Medical School, and in Neuropharmacology at the Biozentrum in Basel Switzerland – he joined the faculty of the Human Genetics Department of Yale Medical School in 1978 as an Assistant, then Associate, Professor.  He became Director of the Division of Developmental Neurobiology at the E. K. Shriver Center in Waltham, MA in 1988. In 1992 he moved to the Departments of Neurosciences and Neurology at Case Western Reserve University Medical School and University Hospitals of Cleveland. While there, he directed the University Alzheimer’s Center from 1999 through 2005. In 2006 he moved to the Piscataway/New Brunswick campus of Rutgers University to become Professor and Chair of the Department of Cell Biology and Neuroscience. In addition to this leadership role in a large public university, he helped to found the Brain Health Institute, a unique public/private partnership devoted to basic research with relevance to clinical neuroscience. In July 2012, he moved to Hong Kong to become the Head of Life Sciences at Hong Kong University of Science and Technology. 

His laboratory research is focused on the biology of nerve cell death and the paradoxical role that failed cell cycle regulation plays in the process. His work includes a strong translational interest that directs his studies towards a few select human neurodegenerative diseases including Alzheimer’s, a very common late-life dementia, and ataxia-telangiectasia, a very rare multisystem disorder of childhood. Dr. Herrup has authored a large number of highly cited papers and until 2010 served as the founding Senior Editor of the Neurobiology of Disease section of the Journal of Neuroscience.

Johannes Gräff, PhD

Assistant Professor
Brain Mind Institute
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://graefflab.epfl.ch/home


Epigenetic priming of memory reconsolidation to attenuate remote fear memories
Remembrances of traumata range among the most enduring forms of memories. Despite the elevated lifetime prevalence of anxiety disorders, effective strategies to attenuate long-term traumatic memories are scarce. The most efficacious treatments to diminish recent (i.e., day-old) traumata capitalize on memory updating mechanisms during reconsolidation that are initiated upon memory recall. Here, we show that in mice successful reconsolidation-updating paradigms for recent memories fail to attenuate remote (i.e., month-old) ones. We find that whereas recent memory recall induces a limited period of hippocampal neuroplasticity mediated, in part, by S-nitrosylation of HDAC2 and histone acetylation, such plasticity is absent for remote memories. However, by using an HDAC2-targeting inhibitor (HDACi) during reconsolidation, even remote memories can be persistently attenuated. This intervention epigenetically primes the expression of neuroplasticity-related genes, which is accompanied by higher metabolic, synaptic and structural plasticity. Thus, applying HDACis during memory reconsolidation might constitute a treatment option for remote traumata.

Biography
Johannes Gräff received his M.Sc. 2005 from the University of Lausanne, for which he focused on deciphering molecular mechanisms of aging in ants in the lab of Laurent Keller. For his graduate studies, he moved to the ETH in Zürich, where he worked on epigenetic mechanism in learning and memory in the laboratory of Isabelle Mansuy and obtained his PhD in 2009. For his postdoc, he moved to MIT in Cambridge, MA, where he started to work on epigenetic mechanisms in neurodegeneration and traumatic memories under the guidance of Li-Huei Tsai. Since fall 2013, he is an assistant professor on tenure track at the Brain Mind Institute at the EPFL. The research focus of his laboratory lies in various aspects of neuroepigenetics.

Ralf Schneggenburger, PhD

Full Professor
Brain Mind Institute
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://lsym.epfl.ch/

Role of BMP signaling in synapse growth and synapse plasticity in the CNS
Neurons communicate via synapses, and synaptic networks are set-up during brain development with exquisite precision and specificity. First, many type of projections neurons can make thousands of output synapses with specific partner neurons often localized in largely separated brain areas. This wiring specificity requires axon pathfinding decisions during brain development, which often involve midline crossing decisions. Second, once an axon has found an appropriate partner neuron, a given synaptic connection can be either strong (large synapse), or weak. However, the mechanisms which determine this synapse strength have only received little attention.

We use a prominent excitatory projection in the lower auditory system, the so-called calyx of Held synapse, to study these questions. These calyx - type synapses in the lower auditory system are characterized by fast firing properties, fast transmitter release, and large synapse size; adaptations that are crucial for preserving the time information inherent in acoustic signals. Using tissue - specific conditional deletion of gene function with the Cre-lox system and Cre driver mouse lines specific for the lower auditory system, we have begun to investigate the function of signaling genes in the specification of synapse development. First, we showed that bone morphogenetic protein (BMP) signaling drives the morphological and functional adaptations at developing calyx of Held synapses (Xiao et al. 2013, Nat. Neuroscience). Second, we found that misrouting axons to the wrong brain side during earlier development results in impaired functional maturation of synapses, indicating that midline crossing conditions functional maturation of large commissural synapses (Michalski et al. 2013 Neuron). These studies show that BMP signaling, besides its well-known earlier roles in cell fate specification and CNS patterning, might be re-used later in brain development to specify synaptic wiring and - plasticity.

Biography
Ralf Schneggenburger obtained a PhD in Natural Sciences at the University of Göttingen in 1993, and was a post-doctoral fellow at the University of Saarland (1994) and at the Ecole Normale Supérieure (1994 - 1996). During further postdoctoral work and as a Research Group Leader at the Max-Planck Institute for biophysical Chemistry (Göttingen, 1996- 2005), he developed a research program in transmitter release mechanisms, and presynaptic plasticity. In 2005, he was appointed as a Professor at EPFL's Brain Mind Institute where he is leading the Laboratory for Synaptic Mechanisms.
