    Careers in NGOs

    Career 6

     

    Careers in non-governmental organizations

     

    Thursday April 3rd, 2014

     

    “ Find out about the people whose current work is focused on strengthening research for health systems, raising society’s awareness of sustainability issues and shaping disease control on a global scale. You will learn what their work entails on a daily basis and about their career path. ”

     

    Invited speakers:

    Dr. Sylvia Generoso (SG) : Project manager at LaRevueDurable (LRD), based in Fribourg, since 2009. LRD is an organization whose main activity is the publication of a magazine on environment and sustainability and, which, in addition, tries to create awareness and empowerment about climate change, through leading various projects in the French speaking part of Switzerland and in France, in partnership with cities, universities, NGOs. Sylvia holds a PhD in atmospheric physics from the University of Paris (2004). She has also previously worked 3 years as a post-doc at EPFL and 6 months as a research associate at International Risk Governance Council in Geneva. In total 6.5 years in academia.

    Dr. Julie Torode (JT) :  Deputy CEO and Advocacy & Programmes Director of the Union for International Cancer Control (UICC). The Union for International Cancer Control or UICC is a membership based, non-governmental organization that exists to help the global health community accelerate the fight against cancer. Prior to joining UICC, Julie did a PhD and a postoc in chemistry and spent the last 10 years in Germany with various roles in the pharmaceutical industry with a particular focus on gynecological oncology.

     

    Dr. Gabriela Montorzi (GM) :  Programme Manager at the Council on Health Research for Development (COHRED). COHRED is an international non-governmental organization whose primary objective is to strengthen research for health and innovation systems, with a focus on low- and middle-income countries. Gabriela holds a PhD in Life Sciences from the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology and a MA in Bioethics and Law from the University of Barcelona.

    The event was well subscribed, with over 100 people signed up, and the speakers faced a well-filled room, with over 60 attendees. All three speakers offered a 20 minute presentation of their career path to date, their personal motivation to work for an NGO, and what their experience in academia brings to their daily jobs. The audience wasn’t shy in asking how to go about getting an internship or a job in the NGO world, and appreciated the opportunity to talk to the speakers at the apéro afterwards.

     

    Sylvia Generoso did her PhD and postdoc in the field of physics applied to modelling atmospheric particle distribution and pollution. Her motivation for leaving academia and turning to NGOs was a strong feeling that the vast amount of knowledge she had accumulated through her academic life wasn’t making an active difference, and that she had little influence on actors that could truly influence climate change.  She started out with a 6-months research associate stint at the International Risk Governance Council in Geneva, an institution aiming to bridge the gap between knowledge progress and policy on climate change. For Sylvia, this meant seeing her research domain from a very different viewpoint. In December, she then joined LaRevueDurable (http://www.larevuedurable.com/fr/)  in Fribourg where she’s been working since. Since this is a small NGO, with 4 people to date (3 journalists), the hats Sylvia wears on a daily basis are very varied. The NGO’s activities revolve around sustainability issues, and go from developing an idea, through writing concepts and proposals, finding partners and fundraising, to implementation on site. Sylvia enjoys the fact that this is such a multifactorial, versatile job. She points out that even though she evidently draws on her academic knowledge, she’s actively pursued additional training during her postdoc and current job that optimized her skills: at EPFL she undertook a career assessment session, a course on Project Management (4 days), on Communication (1 day) and on Fundraising Methods (1 day) who all come in handy now. In addition, she asked for some time off from work to follow a CAS (Certificate of Advanced Studies) on Management of Development Projects at EPFL (http://cooperation.epfl.ch/madepro ). This type of CAS is offered through the Cooperation & Development Center (CODEV) at EPFL, whose aim is to promote and coordinate scientific cooperation activities within EPFL. Typically, courses take 8-10h of personal work per week in addition to monthly classes.

    Although she very much enjoys her current position, Sylvia has not stopped “shopping around” for opportunities.

    Her advice for successful job hunting:

    -sell yourself well

    -convince people that you will not get bored during the job

    -understand what the hiring company is looking for and make sure to convince them by speaking their language

    -understanding what they do and want and how you can give them what they need

    -NO academic CV, softskills are important not your research!!!!

     

    Questions to Sylvia:

    Q:        How does this job in an NGO differ from a postdoc in academia?

    SG:      PhD and postdoc work is very much focussed on a single specific domain and task, while here in the small NGO, there are constantly different people, different tasks and different topics to deal with.

    Q:        In your first NGO job, was it hard to be taken seriously as an academic?

    SG:      No, absolutely not – EPFL is a highly valued as an institution, and holding a PhD and a postdoc are also valuable assets. The main preoccupation of employers is that you might get bored and leave, which is a problem for them, so you should show that you are really interested and that you will stick to it if they hire you.

    JT:        Comment on this: make sure you adapt your CV when applying for a job outside academia – hirers are not interested in reading your latest research, but they want to know what skills you can derive from your experience that would fit their criteria.

     

     

     

     

    Julie Torode presented her tortuous but entertaining career path next. She currently is Deputy CEO for the UICC, the largest and oldest NGO for cancer, based in Geneva (www.uicc.org ). Julie put up her key messages straight away:

    ·      always self-reflect, ask yourself what makes you happy during your daily work

    ·      stay open to new opportunities and don’t be scared to change route if necessary

    ·      follow your gut feeling

    ·      finally, be bold to ask for what you want

    Julie did her PhD in organic chemistry in Liverpool, UK, and was rather taken aback when for her postdoc she moved from a female-friendly environment to being the only woman in a German chemistry lab. Although in her mind she’d always been “on an academic track”, she realized she preferred teaching to lab, and after postdocking went back to the UK where she taught 11-18 year olds in schools. For family reasons, she then moved back to Heidelberg, Germany where her partner was finishing his PhD, but had to learn German to keep teaching. In the meantime, to top up her funds, she was looking for odd jobs and came across an offer to do English translations for a small clinical trials company. She stresses the advantage of working for a small company: you learn more because you get to do more varied things as there are less employees with highly specialized job descriptions. However, the clinial trials held a bit too much variety for Julie’s taste, as she felt she didn’t accumulate enough in-depth knowledge in each field and wanted to specialize in one. She chose oncology, and when Heidelberg-based Astra and Zeneca merged in 2000, she applied “because the offices were just round the corner”. She went to a random interview with her precise dream job in mind, and got offered to move to Hamburg to do this exact job, in clinical trials for oncology with AstraZeneca. After 10 years in Germany, when her partner moved to Geneva with Merck-Serono, Julie followed – knowing she loved her job and wanted to stay in oncology, but not necessarily in BigPharma. She took a couple of months out to reflect and carefully chose a job, and this way found her way to UICC. The UICC is a large NGO whose members include research institutes, governments, hospitals, cancer societies, and patient organizations. UICC sits in Geneva because of the proximity with other organisations like the WHO, UN agencies and other institutions it interacts with. The strenghts of UICC lie in advocating science to global policy makers, organizing congresses, conventions and round tables, and leading discussions for strategic partnerships with funders (private and public) to improve cancer research and treatments.

    So why would UICC recruit a chemistry postdoc/school teacher/clinical trials scientist for deputy CEO? In Julie’s eyes, the skills she brought from each of her previous jobs, as diverse as they may be, were part of what made her the right person for the hirer: communication skills from teaching in schools, pratical knowledge on conducting clinical trials, and scientific insight into oncology.

    Julie’s advice:

    -taylor your pitch to the job when applying, tick the boxes that the employer asks for

    -do not think of yourself as on a committed career path, you determine your future and shape the path along the way

    -when you change your job: experience you have always stays with you and change lets you grow

    -“Happiness in your job makes you excellent in your job!”

     

     

    Gabriela Montorzi joined the Council on Health Research for Development (short COHRED http://www.cohred.org/ ), after university studies in Argentina, a PhD at EPFL,  bioethics studies in Barcelona and a postdoc in Geneva. She says the change from academia to something else was difficult, not least because it took her almost a year and a half to find the right job. After her postdoc Gabrierla was on unemployment benefits, which was helpful in letting her volunteer in unpaid internships. However, quite frequently she found her applications met with refusal because she was overqualified. Her first internship was with the ethics department at WHO and lasted 3 months, after which she had an opportunity, through unemployment services, to train in project management with a job placement in a company where she might want to work afterwards. As she’d already volunteered with WHO, this was not an option. As she wanted to exploit both her bioethics background and her new project management skills, Gabriela contacted the director of COHRED, who happened to need a project manager. He took her on for a 4 months trainig period, again unpaid, but Gabriela has been with COHRED for 9 years since, holding different positions along the way. COHRED was created in 1993 to create equity and socio-economic development from health research. According to Gabriela, working for COHRED today takes faith in your work, because it’s not a job that always brings immediate, tangible results; it takes creativity, because there are little resources for lots of projects; it takes patience because the pace of change on a global scale is slow; and it takes perseverance and focus to keep fundraising. But in return, you get to work with interesting, idealistic people, you learn new skills, explore new areas and multitask on a daily basis; you are part of a small work community which is less structured and hierarchical than academia, so you do have more influence, maybe even the opportunity to change the world!

    Questions for Gabriela:

    Q:        Is it ok to be unemployed while looking for a job? or does it look dodgy on your CV to have a break?

    GM:     I cannot actively job-hunt and keep working full-time for my previous job, I need to put my mind 100% on one task, so personally I prefer to job-hunt while unemployed.

    JT:        It depends on what you do with your unemployment time. Self-reflection periods can be extremely helpful, but make sure that you explain this time-out well when you apply for jobs, and show what you made of it.

     

     

     

    General questions to all speakers:

    Q:        It is a risk to just do another postdoc to “fill time” while waiting for the perfect job – should we refrain from doing this although it pays the bills?

    GM:     You need to ask yourself, am I happy with this job? if not, don’t just stick around postdocking. But again, that’s a personal decision.

     

    Q:        If I want to do some teaching, just for a bit to see whether I like it or whether I’m good at it, is it possible to change back to something else?

    JT:        I immensely liked the teaching itself, but I hated the bureaucracy and the interaction with bitchy colleagues...So ask yourself, is it a conscious step, or am I just drifting into it because it’s an available option?

    Q:        (to Sylvia): How many job applications did you send out, and how many interviews did you get, before landing your job after academia?

    SG:      For the first one, while on unemployment, I found a job ad on an EPFL platform, I applied and got it. For the second one, same, I applied and got it. Since working for LaRevueDurable however, I’ve occasionally applied to interesting offers, but in about 10 applications, I’ve only had 3 interviews and no new job. Some advice on applications/interviews: companies will first check whether you do have all the technical skills they ask for, then they will assess your social skills, and last look for any “plus” you can offer.

    Q:        Are job ads in the NGO sector usually “real” openings, or are they already given internally to someone?

    JT:        “Pretend” job openings are more common in the private sector – NGOs wouldn’t advertise a job if it’s already been filled internally simply because they run on limited funding and can’t afford to waste time and money on fake interviews. That said, if you already volunteered with the company, you’re a much more likely candidate simply because it puts employers at ease to know how you fit in. At UICC, we always need more extra people to help with World Cancer Day, so it’s a great chance to get a foot in the door.

    GM:     At COHRED we don’t advertise fake jobs because it’s too much trouble – for an employer, the aim is to reduce the risk of having to fire someone as it’s disruptive to the team and by far more cost-effective to hire someone that the team already knows.

     

    Q:        How easy is it to get an internship with an NGO? How should we go about it, send spontaneous applications? And how do we convince them to take us?

    JT:        If you’re living close by and the NGO doesn’t need to cover any living expenses, it’s usually easier for the NGO to take you. WHO writes out internships but smaller NGOs might not, so approach them directly and ask.

    GM:     What you bring has to be relevant to us: we have no excess resources, no extra time or people to look after you, but we wouldn’t want an intern just to sit around and learn nothing so we’d only take you if we were sure we had the time/money/people to make it valuable experience for you.

     

    Q:        Do your NGOs consider open applications? Or if you have no use for a person right away, do you file such applications and go back to them later?

    JT:        Small NGOs might retain CVs but they have limited funds, so they’ll be very careful about expanding the team. If the profile of a candidate is likely to come in handy quite soon, the CV will be kept.

    GM:     The pace of work is fast and CVs might disappear, so it’s alwas safer to keep reapplying.

     

    Q:        What are the differences between the private sector and NGOs in terms of applying for positions?

    JT:        In the private sector, the process is longer, there is systematic filtering via the personnel department and along search criteria, so it’s harder to get past this initial hurdle. This is not the case in NGOs.

    GM:     It depends on the size of the company – large NGOs work just like big private companies and vice-versa.

    SG:      The process on the employer’s side might be different, but your job is always the same: to convince them, speak their language, understand what they do and what they’re looking for and sell yourself to fit the bill.

     

    Written by Claudine and Verena.
