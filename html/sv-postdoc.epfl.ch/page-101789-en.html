    Scientific fundraising and management Scientific fundraising and management

     

    Careers in scientific fundraising and management 

    Thursday 31st October 2013

    “  Find out about the people whose careers are focused on supporting and managing the science you love. You will learn what that entails on a day-to-day basis and how to get such a position.”

    Invited speakers were:

    Iain Old:  Project Manager for More Medicines for Tuberculosis (MM4TB), currently based at EPFL

    Sacha Sidjanski:  Coordinator-Development Office, School of Life Sciences at EPFL

    Moira Cockell:  Freelance Scientific Editor at ALWRITE

     

    Over 50 people, to a large majority postdocs, signed up for the event, and we had a decent turn-out with an audience of about 30. Speakers had been asked beforehand to answer a couple of questions about their career path, the reason why they chose this particular career and how/what they liked/disliked about their jobs.

     

    Iain Old  opened the session and traced his path from the University of Edinburgh, where he did a Bsc in Molecular Biology, via a PhD in Nottingham to a rather long stay at the Institut Pasteur in Paris, where he stayed for a total of 18 years before circumstances made him move to EPFL.

    Iain started as EMBO-funded staff scientist in Stewart Cole’s lab, who was to become Senior Vice President for Scientific Affairs. As second to the boss, Iain got rapidly involved with high-profile policy makers, something he had not been expecting or prepared to as a postdoc. But mixing with all sorts of decision makers got him lots of exposure to non-scientist thinking, for example working closely together with deputis from the Center for Disease Control (US) inspecting Pasteur for Health and Safety, or mingling with top-notch funders including the US president. However, as the scientific funding landscape in France got under pressure, the institute underwent significant remodelling after the resignation of the Head. During this brief period of job uncertainty, Iain was offered a job in International Affairs based on his contacts from Pasteur, and shortly considered working at an embassy. However, Stewart Cole moved his tuberculosis lab to EPFL, and Iain followed as European Grants project manager.

    He has been involved in all the FP5, 6 and 7 programmes running in the Cole lab, which are usually large consortia between multinational labs and organisations, and typically move over 10 million Euro per framework programme. Despite having no particular education in business, Iain says he did pick up all the necessary finance and legal knowledge on the job. His latest achievement is to start the brand-new iM4TB Foundation, which will be housed on the innovation campus at EPFL, and which funds R&D into tuberculosis drugs.

    Questions to Iain:

    Which useful skills would you have wanted to carry away from your PhD?

    IO: It was difficult to get thrown in at the deep end, finding himself suddenly with lots of responsibility while representing the Director General (S. Cole). Therefore, he advises us to get some experience on running our own expenses during postdoc.

    Where do you go to for NGO funding?

    IO: During the set-up phase of a project, funding is more difficult, it helps to speak to lawyers and only then approach foundations. The first phase can take a year before things are structured.

     

     

    Sacha Sidjanski  took over from Iain Old and similarly traced his steps to his present job, which is as he calls it, a supporter and facilitator for funding to the Dean (J. Hubbell) for SV. In order to illustrate what his job entails, he showed a quick break-down of the annual EPFL budget: 68% come from federal sources (SNF and Marie Curie or EU funding), which means that the 32 remaining percent need to be sourced elsewhere. This is why private funding is of major importance even to federal institutions like EPFL.

     

    Sacha’s CV follows a deeply seated passion for parasites (and constant fundraising at all positions – he certainly knows to knock on doors!): he starts with a postdoc at NYU where he is told to bring his own 2-year grant. With a first 6-month grant, he went around looking for more and succeeds. His advice: always try to get even a small amount of money, which you can then show to founders to prove your fundability and get more. Also, try small foundations first, even just to rehearse your project pitch, before going to large foundations.

    After 2 short self-funded postdocs, he wants to move to the WHO because he’s motivated by a job revolving around parasites, third world diseases and the possibility of maybe working in Africa. After 16 interviews (persistence pays off!!), one WHO office finally takes him on for a 3-month job as a P4 lab scientist, which eventually leads to a 5-year stay. Sacha’s appreciation of his time at WHO: very bureaucratic but highly enjoyable. He then moves on, looking for something more pragmatic, and works with a HIV/AIDS foundation for a while. The many many rejections he faces during this time are lessons he conveys to us: don’t take anything personally, don’t make assumptions, and always do your best.

    Sacha then moves on to EPFL, where he puts his job description like this: find out who wants to help us, who is interested in us, and convince them to fund us. His daily work revolves around writing proposals that get funded, so what’s in a good proposal? Quoting Doris Buffet, sister of the hugely successful philanthropist Warren Buffet, the four key ingredients are relevance, impact, excellence and success. Project proposals in Sacha’s office are usually written together with a non-scientist to make sure they are readable to a large, non-scientist audience. They should have a lay title (and optionally, a scientific one) with a clear goal, and they ideally also contain a clear figure or schematic depicting what the project aims to achieve. In his experience, the target audience of a project proposal looks at the title, maybe the figure, and certainly the budget, three points that make or break the proposal. Scientists looking for funding should show passion, but be honest about their promises, and show project success.

     

    He stresses that among the myriads of big and small foundations out there, it is not always clear-cut whom to approach, because the goal of foundations need to match the project focus (environmental, social, activist,…), and there is of course competition between projects for the same resources. The most crucial ingredient for fruitful interaction with funders, according to Sacha, is communication. The complementary funding sources he works with are based on excellent mutual relationship between funders and project leaders; he insists that feedback at all stages is important. How does EPFL communicate with foundations? First, it is important to get to know a contact person with the foundation, who is kept informed of the project stages. Second, it is crucial to alert foundations of things that go wrong, because hearing of a crisis from a third person damages the trust between founder and scientist. Third, for any granted (or even not granted) funding, the recipient should follow up with a thank you or even an unsolicited progress report, and make sure the funding source is acknowledged. It helps to ask for feedback after a rejection in order to improve the pitch.

     

     

    Moira Cockell rounded off the session by illustrating how she found herself a freelance editor/writer with her own company, www.alwrite.ch  . Her career path starts off up north like Iain Old’s, with a Bsc in Molecular Biology at Edinburgh University in the 70s. She then moves to the LMB in Cambridge as a research assistant, where she hugely enjoyed the genuine scientific meritocracy that permeated the institute. After a couple of years at LMB, she moves to ISREC as a staff scientist which soon turns into PhD. Although she never had any ambition of running her own lab, she has always been passionate about research and found herself rather skilled at it. To her disappointment, the Swiss research landscape did not expect any intellectual input from research staff, which was the main motivation to do a PhD. As one of the few native English speakers at ISREC at her time, Moira soon found herself a hub for manuscript proof reading and editing, and over the years accumulated experience in writing her own papers and giving talks. As a senior staff scientist, she enjoyed a lot of independence and responsibility, and appreciated forming, along with her PI, a complementary and well-balanced team. However, when the PI moved away from ISREC, family ties meant Moira needed to stay in Lausanne, at which she needed to obtain her own funding to stay active in science.

    Unfortunately, most funding bodies do not yet appreciate the need for grants compatible with Moira’s type of unconventional career track, so she was no longer eligible for most Swiss grants. After a couple of short funded stays in various labs, and the stress of living in monthly chunks of financial safety, Moira assessed her options and decided to turn to writing. Although she has never had to regret her decision, she tells us upfront that being a self-employed, independent writer is not a guaranteed job and is not sufficient for independent living! So how hard is it to get into this career path? First, being self-employed means starting from scratch and building a client base. This can take up to one year before the company is running and producing a full income. Her initial clients were frequently previous scientific links, in CH and abroad. However, the exchange rate of Swiss to external currencies became soon prohibitively expensive, which limited her client base nationally.

    Steps to set up your own writing company could look like this:

    1)     Formulate a business proposal which states what you are, and are not, ready or qualified to do (grant writing, conference talk preparation, manuscript editing, posters, clinical documents for pharmaceutical companies…). Moira for example refuses to ghost-write papers.

    2)     Develop a strong work ethic: say no to work if it risks not meeting your own standards.

     

    What is Moira’s daily work like? As an example, her last project took about 40h on an 80 page FP7 project grant from a multi-lab consortium, which she edited for cohesion.

    Positives about her work: having the sole responsibility for decisions, being flexible about where and when to work, constantly facing new challenges and a stimulating range of assignments, the satisfaction of bringing added value to other people’s work.

    Negatives: time management issues, due to the frequency of urgent deadlines; irregular work periods, no personal interaction with most clients, lots of extra background reading which is not paid.

    She warns us that over time our own acute knowledge in our scientific field will fade, and will be replaced by broader, less in-depth knowledge. Work also evolves from focused scientific writing to more stylistic approaches, such as to render a same topic in different voices for different audiences. Moira’s advice to potential freelance writers: ideally, try to become an institutional writer with a fixed contract. If going freelance is your goal, do at least 2-3 years at a medical writing agency or a science management agency, get on-going training and certifications, and plan ahead, using opportunities to practice during your postdoc. 

     

    Your career blogger: Claudine Neyen
