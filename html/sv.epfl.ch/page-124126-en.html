Friday guest speakers and abstracts

March 14, 2014

 

Toyotaka Ishibashi, PhD

Assistant Professor
Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://life-sci.ust.hk/faculty/Dr.T.Ishibashi/index.html

Transcription Elongation Dynamics of RNA Polymerase II One Molecule at a Time
Transcription of DNA by RNA Polymerase II (Pol II), being the first step in producing proteins that are critical to the survival of the cell, represents a common target for regulating gene expression. Despite many years of intense study, there remain many unresolved questions regarding the physical mechanisms underlying eukaryotic transcriptional regulation. Often, as in the case of epigenetic transcriptional silencing, transcriptional repression of Pol II is mediated through the nucleosome (the histone-DNA complex forming the basic repeating unit of chromatin). It is now well-established that nucleosomes repress Pol II activity by taking advantage of pauses that are inherent to the elongation dynamics of Pol II.
We used a dual-trap optical tweezers instrument to quantitatively describe real-time transcription elongation dynamics.  Here, I will discuss how the histone tails and the specific histone-DNA contacts contribute to the strength of the mechanical barrier posed by the nucleosome during transcription elongation by Pol II. I will also present evidence that the secondary structure of the RNA transcript can modulate the tendency for Pol II to backtrack, and in turn, bias the passage of Pol II through nucleosome. Furthermore, I will also talk how the transcription factor IIF and IIS modulate transcription elongation. These findings shed light on the mechanistic details underlying the control of gene expression by chromatin remodeling and transcription factors.

Biography
Toyotaka Ishibashi obtained his Ph.D at Tokyo University of Science in Japan, where he studied DNA replication and repair system in plants. After his doctoral studies he joined to Dr. Ausio lab in University of Victoria for postdoctoral training. He studied transcription regulation by MeCP2 protein and acetylated nucleosomes. He moved to Dr. Bustamante lab at UC Berkeley to complement his nucleosome and transcription research with single-molecule techniques. He joined the Division of Life Science as an assistant professor in October 2013. His lab is interested in elucidating the role of chromatin structure in transcription regulation using single-molecule techniques such as optical tweezers and atomic force microscope.

David Suter, PhD

Assistant Professor
Interschool Institute of Bioengineering
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://suter-lab.epfl.ch/

Transcription at the single cell level
In both prokaryotes and eukaryotes, gene transcription is believed to occur mainly during short and intense periods referred as to transcriptional bursts, interspersed by silent periods. However, in mammalian cells, the fine transcriptional kinetics of endogenous genes has not been characterized yet. Here, we use a short-lived luciferase reporter to record transcription by live imaging and show that several constitutive and circadian genes are transcribed in a discontinuous manner. We correlated photon emission to absolute protein and mRNA numbers, allowing us to quantitatively describe gene expression in individual cells over time. We combine experimentally determined rates with probabilistic inference to fully specify a minimal biophysical model of gene expression that informs about transcription bursts sizes, transcriptional and gene activity switching rates. These analyses revealed that each gene has a characteristic distribution of gene activity periods over time. The durations of activity periods were exponentially distributed, compatible with stochastic off-switching of a gene after it has been turned on. In contrast, a refractory period of variable duration in the “off” state has to be overcome before genes can be switched on again. Single changes in transcription factor binding site sequences had a pronounced effect on transcriptional kinetics, whereas the pattern of transcriptional bursting was almost unaffected by drugs affecting histone acetylation. In summary, our results suggest that there is a broad spectrum of transcriptional discontinuities in mammalian genes, each gene activity pattern reflecting both stochastic and deterministic processes.

Biography
David Suter studied medicine at the University of Geneva and obtained a MD/PhD in the laboratory of Karl-Heinz Krause in 2007 where he worked on neuronal differentiation of embryonic stem cells. He then did a first postdoctoral training in Geneva with Ueli Schibler, where he developed a new method for high resolution monitoring of transcriptional bursting in single mammalian cells. He was then awarded an advanced researcher fellowship from the Swiss National Science Foundation and joined the group of Sunney Xie at Harvard University, where he developed single molecule imaging of transcription factors in live mammalian cells. Since 2013, he is a Swiss National Science Foundation Professor and Tenure Track assistant Professor at the Bioengineering Institute of the EPFL School of Life Sciences. His research as a group leader focuses on single cell and single molecule approaches to study gene expression during embryonic stem cell differentiation.

Bart Deplancke, PhD

Assistant Professor
Interschool Institute of Bioengineering
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://deplanckelab.epfl.ch/

 

A large-scale transcription factor screen identifies ZEB1 as a key transcriptional component of the adipogenic regulatory network
Adipose tissues are key determinants of whole body metabolism and energy homeostasis. Unraveling the molecular mechanisms underlying adipogenesis is therefore highly relevant from a medical perspective. Moreover, such efforts may also inform basic biology as fat cell differentiation serves as a prime model of cellular differentiation. Our current understanding of the regulatory mechanisms underlying the adipogenic process is centered on the transcriptional cascades driven by the C/EBP protein family and the master regulator PPARγ. To further elucidate the regulatory networks underlying adipogenesis, we performed a large-scale screen in which we overexpressed 734 full-length transcription factors (TFs) in mouse pre-adipocytes and probed their effect on fat cell differentiation. We identified 26 pro-adipogenic TFs, many of which have so far not been linked to this process, and validated the ones showing strongest effects. Thus, we provide a valuable resource for drawing a comprehensive model of the adipogenic regulatory network. In addition, we characterized the top candidate, ZEB1, as being essential for adipogenesis both in vitro and in vivo.  Genomic profiling revealed that ZEB1 controls the expression of the majority of known early and late adipogenic transcriptional regulators, rendering this TF a central transcriptional component of fat cell determination and differentiation.

Biography
Bart Deplancke graduated as a bio-engineer at Ghent University in 1998. After pursuing a Ph.D. in Immunobiology at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign (UIUC), he teamed up for his postdoc with Dr. Marian Walhout at the University of Massachusetts Medical School where he engineered a now patented protein-DNA interaction method to enable the unbiased screening of large regulatory elements of interest such as promoters or enhancers versus an array of TFs in high-throughput fashion. At the end of 2007, he became Assistant Professor in the Institute of Bio-engineering in the School of Life Sciences at the EPFL where he focuses on developing novel experimental and computational tools to improve our ability to elucidate the metazoan gene regulatory code and to examine how variations in this code affect phenotypic diversity. Bart Deplancke is currently also guest professor at Ghent University (Belgium), a recent member of the Swiss Institute of Bioinformatics (SIB), and he co-founded the software company Genohm SA for which he currently serves as a board member.

Zhenguo Wu, PhD

Professor
Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://life-sci.ust.hk/faculty/Prof.Z.Wu/index.html

The Role of STAT3 in Muscle Satellite Cells
Our laboratory previously demonstrated that the JAK/STAT pathway play important roles in regulating myogenic differentiation in cell culture models. It remains unclear whether the pathway regulates muscle satellite cells (i.e., muscle stem cells) in vivo. To address this issue, we generated muscle satellite cell-specific conditional STAT3 as well as JAK1 knockout mouse models. Both mutant mice were viable and the skeletal muscle development appeared normal. However, upon injury, the satellite cell-dependent muscle regeneration was partially defective in the mutant mice. Moreover, when we conditionally deleted STAT3 in the satellite cells of dystrophin-null mice, a mouse model for human Duchenne muscular dystrophy, the compound mutant mice exhibited more pronounced muscle defects, with smaller muscle size, decreased number of muscle satellite cells, increased fibrosis, and enhanced infiltration of CD68+ macrophages. Some mechanistic insights will be provided to explain the role of STAT3 in muscle satellite cells in vivo.

Biography
Dr. Zhenguo Wu received his Bachelor degree in 1986 from Nanjing University in China and his Ph.D. degree in Biochemistry in 1995 from the University of Western Ontario in Canada under the supervision of Dr. George Chaconas. He did his postdoctoral training with Prof. Michael Karin in the University of California at San Diego from 1996 to 1999 to study the MAP kinase-mediated cell signaling. He set up his own laboratory in the Hong Kong University of Science & Technology in 1999.
Dr. Wu’s laboratory has a long-standing interest in elucidating the roles of different intracellular signaling pathways in regulating muscle stem cells and muscle differentiation using both primary and immortalized mouse myoblasts as well as different mouse models. His group has shown that both the p38 MAP kinase and the PI3K/Akt pathways are indispensable for myogenic differentiation by regulating the transcription of the myogenin gene that encodes a key myogenic regulatory factor required for myogenic differentiation. In recent years, his laboratory has focused on the roles of the JAK/STAT pathway in muscle stem cells, muscle differentiation and muscle regeneration. Both muscle stem cell-specific JAK1 and STAT3 conditional knockout mice have been generated in Dr. Wu’s laboratory to facilitate the in vivo study.
Dr. Wu is currently a co-director in the Center for Stem Cell research, and a member in the Center for Systems Biology and Human Health at HKUST.

Tom Hiu Tung Cheung, PhD

Assistant Professor
Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://life-sci.ust.hk/faculty/Dr.T.H.Cheung/index.html

Molecular Regulation of Stem Cell Quiescence
Adult stem cells are unique in their ability to produce differentiated daughter cells while retaining their stem cell identity by self-renewal. The quiescent state of stem cells has long been viewed as a dormant state, but our understanding of the molecular regulation and physiological significance of this state remains limited. Dysregulation of quiescence results in the depletion of the stem cell pool. Deciphering the molecular mechanisms regulating the quiescent state will enable us to better devise approaches for stem cell therapies for degenerative diseases such as muscular dystrophy. Muscle stem cells, or “satellite cells”, are a population of adult stem cells that are primarily quiescent in the absence of injury, making them an excellent model to study stem cell quiescence. We hypothesize that the state of quiescence is a poised state awaiting extrinsic signals for activation. Our data showed that the state of quiescence is actively controlled at the post-transcriptional level by microRNAs. Interestingly, we have identified microRNA-dependent pathways that regulate stem cell quiescence and underlie the functional heterogeneity of adult stem cells. Collectively, our data provides strong support for the hypothesis that the quiescent state is an actively regulated state.

Biography
Dr. Tom Cheung obtained his Ph.D. in the Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry at the University of Colorado at Boulder, USA, where he studied cis-regulatory transcriptional regulation and comparative genomics in mammalian species in Dr. Xuedong Liu lab. During his graduate career, his computer science background allowed him to engage and participate in the development of computational tools in identifying cis-regulatory elements using comparative genomics approach. He then moved to Stanford University to continue his postdoctoral training in Dr. Thomas Rando lab, where he devised purification for muscle stem cell isolation and investigated the molecular pathways that regulate muscle stem cell activation, specifically focusing on post-transcriptional and epigenetic regulatory pathways.
Dr. Cheung joined the Division of Life Science at HKUST in April 2013 and his lab will continue to focus on understanding the biology of muscle stem cell. His lab is interested in using methodologies such as advanced next-generation sequencing and fluorescent activated cell sorting techniques to explore the underlying mechanisms that govern the stemness of muscle stem cells

Daniel Constam, PhD

Associate Professor
Swiss Institute for Experimental Cancer Research
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://constam-lab.epfl.ch/

Imaging and function of proprotein convertases in preimplantation development
The secreted proprotein convertases (PC) furin and Pace4 are implicated in regulating axial patterning and germ layer formation by stimulating the proteolytic processing of the TGFβ-related Nodal precursor during early post-implantation development (1, 2). Live imaging using the transgenic fluorescent reporter substrate CLIP revealed that both Furin and Pace4 are already active before implantation (3). However, Furin and Pace4 are dispensable for blastocyst formation, raising the question whether their earliest functions may be masked by yet another PC. To address this, we performed CLIP imaging in preimplantation embryos lacking Furin and PC7. These experiments revealed that Furin functionally overlaps at the morula stage with PC7. Furthermore, analysis on an outbred genetic background shows that Furin and PC7 regulate blastocyst formation and the polarization of trophectoderm by stimulating E-cadherin precursor processing, but that Pace4 can partially compensate for their loss during subsequent development. Our results identify Furin and PC7 as the principal convertases required for E-cadherin precursor processing and epithelial polarization.

Biography
Daniel Constam studied natural sciences at ETH Zürich. After a doctoral thesis on TGF isoforms in the central nervous system in the lab of Adriano Fontana (Univ. Zürich), he joined Elizabeth Robertson at Harvard University as an EMBO postdoctoral fellow to identify proteases involved in the processing of TGFb-related precursor proteins. In 2000, he became an independent group leader at the Swiss institute for experimental cancer research (ISREC) in Lausanne. He was appointed at the EPFL School of Life Sciences in 2005 and tenured as Associate Professor in 2007. His lab studies the molecular crosstalk between stem cells and their microenvironment that governs pluripotency and patterning in the early mouse embryo. He also investigates how the re-wiring of specific developmental morphogenetic signals disrupts tissue homeostasis in polycystic kidney diseases and in cancer.Dr. Cheung joined the Division of Life Science at HKUST in April 2013 and his lab will continue to focus on understanding the biology of muscle stem cell. His lab is interested in using methodologies such as advanced next-generation sequencing and fluorescent activated cell sorting techniques to explore the underlying mechanisms that govern the stemness of muscle stem cells.

Matthias Lütolf, PhD

Assistant Professor
Interschool Institute of Bioengineering
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://lscb.epfl.ch/page-39795-en.html

A low mitochondrial membrane potential marks self-renewing hematopoietic stem cells
Cellular metabolism is emerging as a crucial regulator of stem cell fate. Accordingly, the low oxygen tension found in some hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) niches in the bone marrow imposes a distinct metabolic phenotype that protects these long-lived and rare cells from high levels of reactive oxygen species (ROS). Although HSC quiescence has for long been associated with low mitochondrial activity, whether mitochondria are involved in specifying HSC fate remains largely unknown. In this talk, I will show that a low mitochondrial membrane potential (ΔΨm) is a unique functional marker of stemness. We purified subpopulations of hematopoietic stem cells (HSC) based on their differential ΔΨm. In vivo long-term multi-lineage blood reconstitution assays show that stemness is unequivocally attributed to cells having low ΔΨm, which was also reflected by striking changes in cell cycle and mitochondrial gene sets. Using ΔΨm we could enrich the most primitive HSC by 2-fold for functional stem cells.  Even under in vitro culture conditions, where the surface marker repertoire does not reliably predict in vivo function, we identified self-renewing HSCs by mitochondrial activity-based read-outs. Strikingly, chemical uncoupling of the electron transport chain conserved HSC self-renewal even under differentiation conditions. Our findings thus establish low ΔΨm as a hallmark of HSC stemness and provide novel avenues for therapeutic manipulation of stem cell fate.

Biography
Professor Matthias Lutolf is Head of the Laboratory of Stem Cell Bioengineering at the Institute of Bioengineering (IBI), EFPL. He was trained as a Materials Engineer at ETH Zurich where he also carried out his Ph.D. studies on the development of a novel class of biomaterials for tissue engineering (awarded with ETH medal in 2004). Lutolf carried out postdoctoral studies at the Baxter Laboratory in Stem Cell Biology at Stanford University on microenvironmental (‘niche’) regulation of somatic stem cells (2005-2007). He started up his independent research group at EPFL with a European Young Investigator (EURYI) award. By interfacing advanced biomaterials engineering, microtechnology and stem cell biology, a major goal in his lab is to uncover mechanisms of stem cell fate regulation; knowledge that will contribute to better ways to grow stem cells in culture and use them for various applications.

Yan Yan, PhD

Assistant Professor
Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://life-sci.ust.hk/faculty/Dr.Y.Yan/index.html

 

Regulation of Mechanical Signals during Tissue Morphogenesis
The Hippo signaling pathway is an evolutionarily conserved pathway regulating a wide range of developmental and cellular processes including growth, differentiation, patterning and cell migration. Importantly, the Hippo signaling pathway is known to respond to regulatory signals from the actin cytoskeleton, consistent with the hypothesis that the Hippo signaling pathway might function to sense and interpret mechanical signals such as tissue shape, geometry and tension to coordinate cell behaviors in a multicellular organism.

We identified beta-Spectrin as a protein required for Hippo signaling activity in a genetic screen for oocyte polarity defects during Drosophila oogenesis. Spectrins are known for linking the actin cytoskeleton to the plasma membrane. In this talk I will focus on discussing the mechanistic link between the formation of stress-fiber-like actin structures and loss of Hippo signaling activity in beta-spectrin mutant cells. Elucidation of beta-Spectrin function in the Hippo pathway will advance our understanding of the cytoskeletal control of the Hippo pathway, one of the key unanswered questions in the field.

Biography
Dr. Yan received her BSc degree from Peking University in 2004. She graduated from Princeton University with a PhD degree in Molecular Biology in 2010. As a graduate student in the laboratory of Dr. Trudi Schupbach, she identified and characterized several previously unknown components in the Notch and Hippo signaling pathways. During her postdoctrol training period with Dr. Chris Doe in HHMI/University of Oregon, she focused on studying the cellular basis of neural stem cell delamination. In 2012 she took a tenure-track assistant professor position in the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology.  Her laboratory is interested in how cells sense and respond to mechanical signals such as tension and stiffness to regulate tissue development and organogenesis.

David Banfield, PhD

Professor
Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://life-sci.ust.hk/faculty/Prof.D.Banfield/index.html

Mechanisms of membrane protein retention in the Golgi
Vesicular-mediated transport of proteins between compartments of the endomembrane network of eukaryotic cells often relies on the presence of so-called sorting signals that facilitate a protein's incorporation into nascent vesicles.  A number of vesicle coat complexes are responsible for generating transport vesicles: clathrin-coated vesicles are generated at the cell surface and endocytic compartments through the action of compartment-specific clathrin adaptors (APs), while the COPII and COPI coats are responsible for generating ER- and Golgi-derived vesicles, respectively.   Vesicle-specific cargo proteins are either bound directly by the protein complexes that drive vesicle formation, or by proteins that function as sorting receptors / intermediaries between these proteins and vesicle coats.  Sorting signals and the sorting receptors that direct proteins into clathrin / adaptin – and COPII-coated vesicles have been relatively well studied, but comparatively less is understood about the mechanism(s) and signals by which proteins are sorted into COPI-coated vesicles at the Golgi.  I will present a synopsis of our recent and current efforts to establish the mechanism(s) by which two abundant classes of integral membrane proteins, the glycosyltransferases and SNAREs, are retained in the Golgi.

Biography
Professor David K. Banfield received his BSc degree from Simon Fraser University (Burnaby, British Columbia) and his PhD (in Biochemistry) from the University of British Columbia.  He then spent 4 years as a Human Frontier Science Program and Canadian Medical Research Council fellow at the Medical Research Council Laboratory of Molecular Biology in Cambridge, England with Sir Hugh Pelham.  For the past 18 years Professor Banfield has been on the faculty of the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology. Professor Banfield’s research is primarily concerned with deciphering the mechanisms of membrane protein targeting and retention in the Golgi.

Jun Xia, PhD

Associate Professor
Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://life-sci.ust.hk/faculty/Dr.J.Xia/index.html

Role of PICK1-ICA69 BAR Domain Complexes in Protein Trafficking: Mechanism and Disease
Mammalian cells are divided by elaborate membrane compartments. Proteins are transported between different compartments via trafficking vesicles. Protein trafficking is tightly regulated to ensure proper function of different proteins. We identified PICK1, a peripheral membrane protein with a PDZ domain and a BAR domain, as an important protein trafficking regulator. The PDZ domain of PICK1 binds to a number of membrane proteins while the BAR domain of PICK1 binds to liposome and couple membrane proteins to trafficking vesicles. In addition, the BAR domain of PICK1 forms tight heteromeric BAR domain complex with ICA69, another BAR domain containing protein. The switch from heteromeric PICK1-ICA69 to homomeric PICK1-PICK1 BAR domain complex provides an important mechanism for vesicle sorting and refinement. In brain, PICK1 interacts and regulates the trafficking of AMPA type glutamate receptor and this regulation is critical to synaptic plasticity, the cellular basis of learning and memory. In pancreatic beta-cells, PICK1 controls the trafficking of insulin granules and its deficiency leads to glucose intolerance and symptoms related to diabetes.

Biography
Dr. Jun Xia received his medical degree in 1993 from Xiangya Medical School of Central South University in China. After obtaining his initial scientific training from the Shanghai Brain Research Institute at Chinese Academy of Sciences, he went to study in the Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine in the United States and received his PhD in Neuroscience in 2001. He joined the Division of Life Science, Hong Kong University of Science and Technology as a faculty member in 2002. Dr. Xia’s is interested in synapse organization and function with implications in learning, memory and psychiatric disorders. He also works on molecular mechanism of protein trafficking and its implication in diseases. His work identified PICK1 as a key regulator of protein trafficking. PICK1 forms heteromeric BAR domain complexes with ICA69 and controls the biogenesis and maturation of dense core vesicles such as insulin granules and proacrosomal granules. Deficiency of PICK1 in mice led to glucose intolerance and other features resembling diabetes.

Robert Z. Qi, PhD

Associate Professor
Division of Life Science
HKUST
http://life-sci.ust.hk/faculty/Dr.R.Qi/index.html

Microtubule Organization and Regulation: The Ends Are the Beginning
As an essential scaffold in the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells, microtubules are dynamic polar filaments with one end often attached to microtubule-organizing centers, such as centrosomes, and the other end located distally from the organizing centers and usually fast growing. At the organizing centers, microtubule nucleation and organization require γ-tubulin, a protein that exists in a macromolecular complex called the γ-tubulin ring complex (γTuRC).  At microtubule distal ends, a group of proteins accumulate to regulate microtubule dynamics and attachment to various subcellular structures.

Our current research investigates the organization and regulation of the γTuRC and characterizes proteins that track microtubule distal tips. CDK5RAP2 is a human microcephaly protein that is involved in microtubule organization on centrosomes and microtubule regulation at the distal ends. On centrosomes, CDK5RAP2 associates with the γTuRC and regulates microtubule nucleation. At microtubule distal ends, CDK5RAP2 in association with EB1 modulates microtubule growth and dynamics. These findings would expand our understanding of the molecular principles governing dynamic microtubule organization in various cellular events and how defects in these mechanisms result in diseases.

Biography
Dr. Robert Qi obtained his Ph.D. degree in Biochemistry and Molecular Biology at The University of Calgary, Canada. Prior to joining The Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, he held appointments in the Institute of Molecular and Cell Biology, Singapore as a Principle Investigator, Proteomics Group and Protein Chemistry Facility. The current research of Dr. Qi’s group focuses on understanding the molecular mechanisms that regulate microtubule cytoskeletal organization during cell division, migration, and differentiation, and how defects in these mechanisms result in diseases such as cancer and neurodegenerative diseases. His work applies biochemical, mass spectrometric, molecular and cell biology methods to the study of these fundamental questions in cell biology.

Pierre Gönczy, PhD

Full Professor
Swiss Institute for Experimental Cancer Research
School of Life Sciences
EPFL
http://gonczy-lab.epfl.ch/

 

Mechanisms of centriole assembly
Centrioles and basal bodies are essential for the formation of cilia, flagella and centrosomes, and are characterized by a nine-fold radial symmetric arrangement of microtubule triplets. We, as well as others, have identified components essential for centriole formation across evolution, including the SAS-6 family of proteins. Our work led us to propose a structural model for centriole assembly in which nine homodimers of SAS-6 proteins associate to form a ring from which radiate nine spokes. This model is supported by cryo-electron tomography data in which we investigated the architecture of the exceptionally long centriole of the flagellate Trichonympha, as well as by FCS and 3D-STORM experiments conducted in human cells. Together, these approaches help us unravel the mechanisms at the root of the near universal nine-fold radial symmetry of centrioles.  

Biography
Pierre Gönczy obtained his PhD from The Rockefeller University (New York, USA) in 1995 and joined the laboratory of Tony Hyman at the EMBL (Heidelberg, Germany) as a postdoctoral fellow in 1996. He started his own laboratory at ISREC in 2000 and, in 2005, became Associate Professor at the EPFL School of Life Sciences.
