            RESEARCH ACTIVITIES

            MIR focuses its research activities on three core areas:  1) the co-evolution of institutions and technology, 2) the regulation and governance of network industries and 3) the governance of urban systems.

            (1) The co-evolution between institutions and technology in infrastructures

            Research in this area is conducted since 2005. The idea is to establish an empirically verifiable link between, in the various infrastructures sectors, between a given state of technology, a state of institutional governance, and performance (in these infrastructures). This activity is pursued within two research teams, one external (TU Delft) and one internal (Chair MIR) and has led to the publication of one book, so far, and numerous journal articles.

            (2) Network industry regulation and governance

            The economic regulation of the network industries in the communications, the transport, the energy, and the water sectors is the area of research MIR is best known for. In the past MIR has mainly worked on economic regulation of the postal, the telecommunications, the electricity, and the water sectors. This activity is currently being expanded to the area of transport (rail, road, air, maritime) in collaboration with the European University Institute, Florence School of Regulation, Transportation Area.                   

            (3) Governance of urban systems

            Within this area of research the aim is to analyse key urban infrastructure sectors such as transport, energy, water and sewage, and telecommunications in order to determine efficient and effective governance. For this purpose the role of the most relevant actors for such governance (public, private, third sector) is explored, as well as their optimal relationships in terms of performance and the role intelligent systems can play to improve these relationships. The project is taking place in collaboration with the Stevens Institute of Technology.

             

             

            PHD PROJECTS


            Nicolas Crettenand

            "The facilitation of mini and small hydropower in Switzerland through institutional instruments"

            Sustainable development is a top priority worldwide today. Switzerland, with the forecasted electricity gap between domestic production and demand, aims to significantly increase renewable energy sources (RES), including hydropower. Mini hydropower (<1 MW) and small hydropower (1-10 MW) currently have considerable unused technical and ecological potential. Their energy payback ratios are significantly higher and the production costs generally lower than other RES. The technology is well developed and requires adequate institutional frameworks (e.g. regulation, reducing transaction costs, financial incentives) to maximize its remaining potential under economically viable conditions. One approach to this problem is to develop adequate institutional instruments based on the literature of co-evolution between institutions and technologies. This research takes into account the current liberalisation of the electricity sector, the government's aim to increase the importance of RES and the post-Kyoto discussions on climate change. A special focus is given to  small-scale storage and pump-storage schemes. The aim of this PhD is to develop institutional instruments to facilitate mini and small hydropower in Switzerland - instruments which can be adapted to other countries.

            Christof Duthaler

            "Next-generation capacity allocation in the European Internal Electricity Market: A role model for the future interaction between power exchanges and transmission system operators."

            During recent years, the integration process of national electricity markets towards a true Internal Electricity Market in Europe gained more and more momentum. Under regulatory and political guidance, two crucial institutional players shape this development in an important way: On one side, there are Transmission System Operators (TSOs) responsible for a secure grid operation and related tasks. On the other side, there are power exchanges (PXs) providing an efficient market operation. While the operational area of a TSO has traditionally been confined to a specific country (or part of a country), national PXs have recently commenced the formation of supra-national exchanges covering larger areas of Europe, a trend most likely to be continued in the upcoming years. When an ever increasing and more European-wide trading activity meets the physical reality of the underlying electricity grid, the attention turns to capacity allocation, i.e. how to make (scarce) grid capacity available to market participants. This is where my thesis comes into play: Acknowledging that traditional capacity allocation methods may face inherent limitations in the new European context and considering the latent danger of a disconnect between market and system operation, my research work is about future allocation methods and the corresponding involvement of TSOs and PXs. Based on insights previously gained in other electricity markets (e.g. US markets), I concentrate on three different layers of capacity allocation, namely a technological one (e.g. grid model requirements and physical constraints), an economical or financial one (e.g. macroeconomic efficiency and microeconomic incentives, handling of financial streams), and finally an institutional one (e.g. scope and distribution of competencies). The goal of the thesis is to develop a role model for the future interaction between power exchanges and system operators in Europe, thereby answering specific questions belonging to these three layers.

            Anne Greinus

            The liberalization process in the European rail sector is developing. While the charges for the use of the rail infrastructure normally do not cover all costs, the deficit is paid by public funds. Funding could be guaranteed by so called multi annual contracts (performance agreements) which shall set incentives for the infrastructure manager to reduce the costs of infrastructure service provision. However, the regulation and organisation of the railway sector may have an impact on these economic incentives. E.g. a vertical integrated railway is not only an infrastructure manager but also railway undertaking and sometimes acts also as a real estate company.

            Eva Lieberherr

            "Transformation of Governance in the Water Sector: An Assessment of Performance in Switzerland, Germany and Great Britain."

            The transformation from state-centered policies to new, non-hierarchical forms of coordination between political, economic and civil societal actors has led to new modes of governing in utility sectors in Europe since the 1980s. Such restructuring under the umbrella of liberalization has led to critical questions regarding the definition, control and organization of a public service (e.g. water supply). The term "governance" is used in the social sciences to refer to an interactive process where public and private actors coordinate and cooperate in developing, adopting and implementing policies. Furthermore, the governance concept provides a means through which problems induced by complex political, economic and societal challenges as well as new modes of coordination between public and private actors can be researched. Utilizing the governance perspective, this research seeks to examine and explain the strengths and weaknesses of different governance and organizational modes in the water supply and wastewater treatment sectors and thus evaluate their performance. In order to conduct a comparative assessment of the diverging forms of governance in the European context, three countries that have taken varying approaches to (re-)structuring their water sectors are selected for analysis: Switzerland, Germany and Great Britain. While Switzerland may represent a case with a neutral attitude towards liberalization in the water sector, Great Britain is regarded as characterizing a strong liberalization approach. Germany lies in between these two countries as it deploys a mixture of private and state-centered governance modes in the water sector. The overall objective of this research is to shed light on how the transformation of the state and the emerging governance forms in the water sector affect the allocation of such a public service.

            Martin Maegli

            "Regulatory Institutions in the Postal Sector: The Behavior of Regulatory Authorities and the Consequences for Institutions and Costs of Governance in Regulatory Arrangements."

            The liberalization of the postal sector and its implementation are currently important issues in Switzerland as well as in the European Union. This liberalization process is accompanied by new institutional policies resulting in the creation of new actors, notably independent regulatory authorities. Different countries exhibit different institutional arrangements in regulatory matters, which in turn lead to different regulatory practices. We assume that regulatory governance has a significant cost which we define as governance costs of a regulatory system. The various actors in network industries relate to each other by way of formal and informal rules called institutions. The maintenance of these regulatory institutions, as well as their daily activities, have a cost, which we describe as costs of regulatory governance. The overall costs of regulatory governance are still largely underestimated or even ignored to a large extend. Theorists as well as practitioners do so far not pay attention to these particular costs of a regulatory system. These costs are inherently present in regulatory arrangements and influenced (1) by the behavior of the various actors and (2) by their institutional design within a regulatory framework in unequal ways. The dissertation is focusing in particular on the behaviour of regulatory authorities in the postal sector. Therefore the empirical research stresses the identification and analysis of regulatory regimes as well as the regulator's incentives and drivers to act strategically vis-à-vis different actors. It assesses how regulators behave and try to shape their relevant institutional framework, and how the particular behavior influences governance costs in regulatory arrangements. The contribution of the research project therefore will be to integrate newly created sector specific regulatory authorities in the institutional framework of regulation.

            Toni Männistö

            “Institutional Roles in Global Supply Chain Security – Case European Union”

            Global supply chains are threatened by a variety of security hazards. Cargo theft, smuggling of prohibited goods, counterfeiting, sabotage, and terrorism,  are a few examples from a long list of supply chain crime types that concern supply chain managers and government authorities alike. Consequences of these illicit activities are far-reaching and severe: compromised citizen safety, lost tax revenues, unfair competition, degrading environment, and increased corruption, just to begin. To address the problem, governments have adopted a series of anti-crime and counter-terrorism policies and refocused their law enforcement activities. Companies have been active in developing and deploying new technologies and operational procedures to protect their cargo, employees and reputation from crime. Even so, criminal activities in the global supply chains are on the rise. Companies often claim that many government-origin supply chain security regulations increase more costs and administrative burden than contribute to security. Moreover, multiple academics and practitioners have highlighted that governmental anti-crime and anti-terrorism efforts in the transport sector are sub-optimal in many ways. Especially a lack of international and cross-functional collaboration and coordination impede authorities to pursue and dismantle supranational criminal and terrorist networks.
            This thesis project has three goals. The first one is to identify, describe and classify all criminal activities involved in the international supply chains. The second goal is to show how the European Union as an international institution addresses supply chain crime problems. The third goal is to answer how the European Union could respond effectively and efficiently to supply chain crime threats in the future.

             

            RESEARCH EXCHANGE PROGRAM

            MIR is offering a number of Visiting Fellowships and invites applications from Switzerland and abroad.

            Fellows will normally be researchers with a proven track record in MIR's area of research.

            Visiting Fellows will be provided with office space. It is expected that Fellows will contribute to MIR's seminar and collaborate with MIR researchers either on a publication or research project.

            Applicants should send by e-mail a statement of their proposed research project, their CV, and an indication of when they wish to visit.
