    • BNF

      23 October 2014

      BNF event

       

      From the BNF website:

       

      BNF is a national qualification program offering temporary employment to highly qualified job seekers of all ages to open up professional perspectives. BNF offers project placements in the regular job market and a support package of expert advice, complementary training and personal coaching. BNF is active throughout Switzerland. Job seekers from all regions may participate in project work in a location of their choice. BNF has branches in Bern, Basel, Zurich and Lausanne.

       

      Speaker: Dr Alexandre Noël

      Dr Alexandre Noël started out by reassuring us that he had “been there, done that”: yes, his career includes a PhD and research stints, and yes, he’s been unemployed, even more than once. But according to him, even if you end up jobless at some point in your life, this doesn’t mean you’ll never get a job again.

      BNF originally stands for “Biomedizinische und Naturwissenschaftliche Forschung” (Biomedical and Life Sciences Research) but has since its creation in 1996 moved away into virtually any domain YOU want to work in. Its aim is to promote networking and to develop and improve employability in highly skilled job leavers, frequently PhDs and postdocs, but also highly qualified personnel from the pharma industry, for example. BNF acts under a mandate of the Swiss State Secretariat for Economic Affairs (SECO). This means that BNF, although originally a university spin-off, is bound by federal laws on employment (the Unemployment Insurance Act), and also depends on SECO on the amount of money they can distribute through placements. It also means that placements can only be carried out in organisations that meet a certain number of criteria.

      So what to do when you are facing unemployment?

      First, be aware that all unemployment issues are handled on a cantonal/local basis and will require you to work your way through a lot of paperwork – in the cantonal language. If English is your first and only language, try to get help to navigate the forms and admin with a native speaker.

      Your main point of contact is your local ORP branch (Office Régional de Placement – Local placement office) and your Caisse de Chômage (Cch). The ORP proposes job market measures (Mesures relatives au marché du travail, MMT) which are supposed to improve your reinsertion chances, and also controls your job search. The problem with MMTs is that they are not ideally targeted to a highly skilled audience, and this is where BNF comes in, as an external counselling structure.

      BNF proposes (and lets you propose) subsidized job placements, temporary measures, or internships at a highly skilled level, and also offers coaching with obtaining these positions. Projects can include research in labs and in clinical settings, but there are also a number of internships in companies available (this is a more recent development, over the last 2 years, and is more difficult to set up because of less flexible internal structures in companies). And you may of course create your own project, which needn’t even be in Switzerland if there is reasonable ground for it.

      How do you get access to BNF?

      1)      Register as unemployed at your local ORP (and ONLY your local ORP - unemployment outside Switzerland does NOT get you access to BNF!)

      2)      Obtain the agreement of your ORP counsellor to contact BNF

      3)      Work out with BNF what kind of project you might want to pursue – here the law clearly states that you should NOT use your job placement to learn something completely new and unrelated, but as the aim is to improve your employment perspectives, you can still try to argue your case and branch out a bit. Another legal point: MMTs should be adapted to the current situation on the job market – if you propose a project in something very obscure that will not improve your employability, it will not be accepted.

      4)      Be motivated and show initiative – BNF cannot and will not push you or force you to take up a project. It is in your hands to contact the project leader.

      5)      BNF draws up a contract between you and the project leader and the ORP licenses the procedure.

      6)      If you wish, follow up with BNF, they remain at your disposal for further career advice.

      7)      BNF placements clearly state that you need to set aside time for job search, and they also explicitly do not want you to work overtime, extra hours and weekends so that you can fully concentrate on finding a new job. Once you find a job, the BNF placement stops immediately. Even though BNF placements are not generally thought as a foot in the door to a company, 70% of job seekers on a BNF placement find a job, and among these 15% stay with the organisation of their placement.  

      What are the goals on a BNF placement? With their programme, BNF aims to

      • facilitate professional reintegration
      • reinforce motivation and help job seekers keep their spirits up
      • improve application skills (CV, interviewing)
      • find individual solutions (this includes helping you create your dream internship!)
      • if possible, create new projects and workplaces
      • reduce the number of people on long-term (over 6 months) unemployment
      • enable unemployed job seekers to improve their skills while job-hunting

      Even though BNF now sounds like a thrilling thing to try out, how do you avoid getting unemployed in the first place?

      1)      Do everything possible to prevent unemployment (long enough in advance) and most importantly, keep the proofs (this can include informal email exchange). The ORP will want these proofs to see you’re worthy of unemployment benefits.

      2)      Know the administrative procedures. And if you don’t read administrative French/German/Italian, have someone help you.

      3)      Register with your local ORP well ahead of the end of your contract (weeks to months).

      4)      Have a strategy and get yourself organized.

       

      Special cases include:

      BSc or Master graduates: since they have not been working, they have not paid in unemployment insurance and therefore have to face a waiting period of 120 days before being paid unemployment benefits, which are restricted to 90 days.

      The same applies for Swiss nationals or foreigners who had resided in Switzerland for at least 10 years but have been working abroad before returning to Switzerland unemployed. So think twice before doing your postdoc abroad…Swiss unemployment might pay you better than a postdoc in many EU countries…

      While on the 120-day wait, you will also have no access to MMTs (includes BNF) but you are allowed to take courses.

      Sabbatical: a sabbatical does not count towards your work time. In order to get full unemployment benefits, you need to have worked for 2 years, so avoid taking a 6-month sabbatical at the end of your 2-year fellowship as it might destroy your chances to benefits considerably.

      Permit issues: since your permit may depend on your work contract and your unemployment rights depend on your permit, make sure you renew the permit in time.

       

      Why do a BNF placement?

      The use of an MMT can be manifold – it might give you the chance to learn/improve a language that you need for a job in a different canton, or it might help you avoid having a gaping hole in your CV, or it might help you to focus your job search by trying out options. But in any case, Dr Noël advises us to be selfish, to think carefully before doing a BNF placement, because there are two sides to being unemployed.

      Negatives are easy to think of, unemployment means a drop in finances, it’s bad for your mental state and can lead to pressure, isolation and frustration, and leave unseemly holes in your CV. But, on the positive side, being unemployed gives you time to self-reflect, take stock of your skills and explore new options and social environments. And last but not least, it’s a great time for taking risks, since you have nothing else to lose!

       

      Questions for Dr Noël:

       

      Q? Are BNF placements restricted to the Life Science/biomedical domains?

      No, the topics are very broad, the common denominator is “highly skilled” and the consultants at BNF have very varied backgrounds themselves.

       

      Q? How much shift is required between the last job and the placement, how free are we to explore/learn new skills?

      BNF is trying to be more flexible than the law, usually a solution can be found that complies with your wish to develop and the law.

       

      Q? What is the time between contacting BNF and getting an interview?

      Time varies but at the moment we are quite busy, it can take at least 3 weeks before you get an appointment. But in cases where you already have a placement offer we hurry up the procedure.

       

      Q? how does being on unemployment benefits affect starting your own company?

      This is something to check carefully with your ORP; the common procedure is that you get 3 months off where you receive no benefits and you try to get the company going, and after these 3 months you can still get back to unemployment benefits where you left off.

       

      Q? Related to preventing unemployment: postdocs are no longer junior enough to be eligible for industry internships, but they also struggle to get senior positions because these typically ask for industry experience. Should we still do BNF placements and does this improve our chances?

      Yes, you should certainly do a BNF placement, because the skills you build count towards your “industry experience”, however minimal. BNF encourages you to suggest a project with a company that you’re interested in working for, that way you may even increase your chances of being hired by them. In that line, it makes sense to register with both the ORP and BNF before the end of your contract and get in touch with the company where you want to carry out your project, so that the transition goes smoothly and you avoid a gap in your CV.

       

      Q? is BNF available to non-EU members?

      Yes as long as you have the right to unemployment, BNF is open to everyone. Again, your first point of contact is your local ORP and Cch.
