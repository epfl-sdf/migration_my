Projets SIE & Master 2013/2014

Utilisation de capteurs passifs pour la determination de micropolluants dans les cours d'eaux

 Organic micropollutants are present in the environment at very low concentrations and it is necessary to use sophisticated methods for their sampling and analysis. For water analysis it is in general necessary to take large volumes of samples. These are difficult to handle, to stock and to extract. Moreover, single sampling will only give an immediate picture of the situation, and not an overall view.

Trying to overcome these problems, passive samplers (PS) have been developed since the 1980s. The idea is to use a device that can be installed in the field (water system) for many days and that concentrates the researched compounds for later analysis in the laboratory. Ideally, passive samplers are cheap, easy to handle, don’t need electrical power to work or don’t need specific survey. To date, many PS were developed, for all kinds of compounds, organics (polar and non-polar) and metals, for different matrices, such as air, water or soils. The most popular for aquatic studies are SPMD (semi-permeable membrane devices), Chemcatchers, POCIS (Polar organic chemical integrative sampler), LDPE (low-density polyethylene) and silicone membranes.In our laboratory, we used LDPE and silicone membranes to monitor PCBs in 2 rivers, the Venoge (VD) and the Birse (JU).

The goal was to find possible sources of these compounds along the rivers and identify hot-spots. Previous work on fish and sediments of both rivers shown a contamination by PCBs, but no identified source was known. By placing membranes inside the water stream, at different locations, it was possible to identify former landfills as probably responsible for this chronic pollution of the waters and subsequently, of fish. The idea for this proposal is to apply passive samplers to measure polar compounds, like pharmaceuticals in river waters.

The reduction of uranium in soil from Kazakhstan by native microbial communities

Keywords: Environmental engineering, remediation, Biostimulation, microbiology, column studies, geochemistry, Kazakhstan, uranium, metagenomics.

Description: 300 m below the vast deserts of Southern Kazakhstan, acidic in situ mining of uranium supplies the world with energy using safer techniques than ever before. But what happens to the acid after mining stops? We hypothesize that bacteria will repopulate the affected areas and will raise the pH, and that these bacteria can be fed to coerce them to grow faster. This proposed project will investigate the rate and efficacy of biostimulation with respect to pH and uranium removal, using laboratory column studies.

Skills to be learned: Environmental chemistry lab techniques, column setup and maintenance, experimental design, report writing, and giving presentations.

Supervisors: Brian Reinsch, Ph.D. (postdoc), Pierre Rossi, Ph.D. (scientist), Rizlan Bernier-Latmani, Ph.D. (professor), and Luiz Felippe De Alencastro, Ph.D. (MER)

Uptake of estrogens into polar samplers

When estrogenic compounds are present in rivers or lakes they can induce effects in for example fish (intersex). Therefore, water quality standards are being discussed for estrogens and there is a need to monitor these compounds. Passive samplers such as the Chemcatcher or the POCIS can be used to do this but uptake into these samplers needs to be understood. At the Eawag in Dübendorf there is a calibration system that can be used to study uptake. In the system, water flows though a channel, this water can be spiked with estrogens and water concentration can be monitored with an autosampler.

After quantification of estrogens in the different compartments “water – membrane – and sorbet “, by means of LC-MS/MS and bioassays for estrogens, uptake into the different compartments can be modelled to parameterise the various processes. Part of the work will be done at the Ecotox Centre at the Eawag in Dübendorf, part of the work will be done at EPFL.

Contacts: Etienne Vermeirssen, Felippe de Alencastro
 

Sources and fate of microplastics in Lake Geneva / Sources et devenir des microplastiques dans le Lac Léman

Plastic and microplastic (plastic particles smaller than 5 mm) water pollution receives growing public attention, as the “garbage patches” or plastic soup and impacts on birds and wildlife become better known. The occurrence of these microplastics has been demonstrated in Lake Geneva, and is currently being investigated in other Swiss lakes by GR-CEL. The distribution, nature, and impacts of the microplastics in Swiss surface waters are studied, but the sources of the particles are yet to be more precisely defined as well as their behavior and transport once in surface waters.

Tasks:
- sampling of sewage, runoff water, rivers, sediments
- analysis of the particles types and degradation
- investigation of the fate of the particles
This project is part of a broader study on microplastics in Switzerland’s surface waters, addressing hot issues of worldwide environmental research.

Contacts: Florian Faure, Felippe de Alencastro

Bioaccumulation models for sediment risk assessment

Context
Many contaminants that enter the environment end up either bound to suspended matter or accumulated in sediments. From there, they are available for uptake and cause toxic effects to aquatic organisms either directly or through secondary poisoning through food ingestion. Risk assessment for chemical substances and contaminated sites requires the consideration of the sediment compartment, however the level of development and implementation of new tools for exposure and effect assessment is still rather scarce compared to the water compartment.
Sediments have several characteristics that make them a challenging environmental compartment to study. The large heterogeneity in its nature and composition encompasses a large variability in bioavailability, challenging the development of environmental quality standards and effect concentrations for effective risk assessment. For a single species, it is predicted that a large range of effect concentrations may be measured for sediments having the same total concentration depending on sediment properties. A proposed alternative is to use as dose metrics a measure of internal exposure such as bioaccumulation because, for a contaminant to cause toxicity, it is assumed that it must be first taken up and accumulated by the organism.
For most organisms living in close contact with the sediment overlying water, pore water, sediment contact and diet all contribute to some extent to the total load of a chemical substance in the body of the organism. Thus effective risk assessment for the sediment compartment requires models that are capable of aggregating exposure through all relevant routes, taking into consideration species-specific natural history and biological features, and site-specific physico-chemical characteristics. Conceptual models using the concept of biodynamics have been developed that are capable of capturing the variability in bioaccumulation and toxicity for different species and scenarios. Such models have shown great potential, still they need further refinement and validation.
Objective
The objective of this master project is to develop further the concept of biodynamics for improving effect evaluation in risk assessment for the sediment compartment. Specific research questions that can be addressed include identifying relevant organisms, chemicals and exposure scenarios, which will lead to further calibration and validation of proposed species- and contaminant-specific models. The results of the project should provide input to the development of integrative methods for toxicity assessment
Required competencies
The project will involve some field, laboratory and desk work covering a broad range of disciplines, from environmental chemistry to ecology. The student should have an interest in gaining hands on experience in lab and field work, and pursue career in the study of the fate and effects of chemicals in the environment.
