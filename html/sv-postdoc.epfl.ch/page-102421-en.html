    Science communication and outreach

    Career 5

     

    Careers in science communication and outreach

    Thursday 21 November 2013

    “ Find out about the people whose careers are focused on communicating science to broader audiences. You will learn what that entails on a day-to-day basis and how to get such a position.”

    Invited speakers were:

    Tabitha Innocent, Scientific Liaison officer, Sense about Science, London, UK

    Farnaz Moser, Equal Opportunity officer, EPFL.

    Delphine Ducoulombier, Science Communicator, Public Laboratory l'Eprouvette, UNIL.  

     

    Clearly our advertising department had done a great job in reaching out to the community, since over 50 postdocs and PhD students signed up for this event. The three speakers did not disappoint their audience and gave a fun and communicative insight into their jobs.

    Farnaz Moser joined EPFL already during her studies while doing a PhD in chemical engineering. She then stayed on at EPFL in the Department of Physical chemistry, working on soil pollution and environmental science for 6 years. For her, a “reorientation” into communication came naturally as she’d always had “2 lives”, being involved in political and social aspects of EPFL life. In her role as president of the EPFL scientific staff association, she was instrumental in getting the first day care set up, launching the careers centre and organising career planning tools for scientific staff. When she was asked to put together an equal opportunities program for EPFL, this quickly grew into what is now the Equal Opportunities Office at EPFL (founded in 2004), of which Farnaz is director.

    Her interests lie in raising awareness of girls of technical studies and promote science to youngsters in general. Outreach activities organized through the Equal Opportunities Office therefore aim at making science accessible to the general public, and to facilitate the public understanding of science. With her small team at the Equal Opportunities Office, Farnaz organizes science clubs and cafés, courses, workshops, exhibitions for different age groups (7-15), science fairs, a science tour bus and campaigns with interactive experiments in schools. Examples you can read about on their website (http://egalite.epfl.ch/) include “Internet pour les filles”, the super-popular Robotics workshop on the EPFL campus, and “Maths en jeu”.

    Farnaz’ job advice: opportunities vary depending on the country; Switzerland doesn’t shine with relatively few opportunities and scarce resources put aside to science communication. On a daily basis, Farnaz’ job therefore revolves around getting funding and submitting proposals.

    Questions for Farnaz:

    Q: How many people work at your office?

    FM: 1 scientific staff with expertise in mediation, 10 staff members of which most are part time, and none has a permanent position.

     

    Q: Are scientists willing to take part in outreach activities?

    FM: Yes, they are generally enthusiastic but language is a barrier, kids only speak French.

     

    Q: Any positive results you’d like to point out?

    FM: There has been an increase in the number of female science students but it’s still early days to say whether these are from Switzerland and because of ongoing efforts since 2004. Many females students at EPFL are indeed from abroad. There is a change of mentality required in Switzerland to get more girls into technical studies. But kids always ask for more so the activities are successful, and the feedback from parents is highly positive too, because they see a positive change in their daughters’ attitude towards science.

     

    Q: How many scientists like us could get your job?

    FM: I don’t have numbers for Switzerland, it’s in essence a job you have to create for yourself.

     

    Q: Which job/project has been dearest to your heart?

    FM: Setting up the daycare, Internet for girls – it was an enormous initial hurdle to justify a girls-only workshop but it’s now hugely successful.

     

    Q: Why doesn’t the federation invest more in science communication?
    FM: There is a need for cantonal and federal collaboration which isn’t present yet, or the problem itself isn’t really recognized yet.

     

    Q: What skills are you looking for in applicants?

    FM: Experience in science education and mediation – as no specific course (at Master level for example) exists in Switzerland yet (it does in France), these skills need to be acquired by doing activities in parallel to your normal curriculum.

     

    Delphine Ducoulombier works at Éprouvette ( http://www.unil.ch/interface/page18617.html), the public lab of UNIL since 2007. On a daily basis, this entails looking after anyone who comes in to try out science. How did Delphine get there? She did a PhD in Cellular and Molecular Biology in France, and at the time was already doing a lot of science communication, based on her interest in linking ongoing research with societal issues. She started taking part in CIPAST (Citizen Participation in Science and Technology, http://www.cipast.org/ ), where she became scientific manager for a duration of 3 years. She then worked at the Cité des Science in La Villette (Paris) then for INRA, and enjoyed being surrounded by social scientists because of their refreshingly different way of thinking. During her work here she met the director of the Science/Society Interface at UNIL, heard about Éprouvette and joined.

    Éprouvette is a tool of the science/society interface department and encompasses a public lab, the Anthropos café to foster exchange of knowledge, and the Réseau Romand Science et Cité. Éprouvette also runs research and teaching for schools. Delphine’s work is to provide direct access to the lab on a daily basis, teach how science is done, keep direct contact with the researchers that help out at Éprouvette, publicise research, organize activities for the tour bus that goes to schools…50% of the public at Éprouvette is made up of schools visiting, the other 50% are anyone who wants to try out science, from age 0 to 99. Daily activities include benchwork to organize the lab, give demonstrations, mentor the other science communicators, update experiments and research practices. All in all, it’s very diverse, with duties ranging from training teachers, developing kits for teaching in schools, visiting schools, attending events and fairs, liaising with patients associations on communication days, mentoring and setting up other hands-on labs and many more. Delphine enjoys the diversity of collaborations: with museums, researchers, other universities, science centres, criminal science schools…The themes that are top-most on Eprouvette’s list are genetics and microbiology, but more niche fields can of course be explored.

    Delphine’s advice for us: there are huge needs for this kind of jobs, but there are only very few permanent positions out there. If you are interested, try to meet people in the field, know what your values are in a job, and be aware that communication jobs can be on many levels, for example at scientific journals, to kids in schools, or to a broad public. Is there a degree needed? Not necessarily, it is far more important to get experience, and to hone your skills by interacting with people from very different fields explaining “your” science. Create your own job, find institutions that support science and society interaction.

    Questions for Delphine:

    Q: Does Éprouvette also communicate with policy makers/politicians?

    DD: Yes, sometimes, but this is a domain that could be more developed.

     

    Tabitha Innocent never planned to go into science communication, and kind of still wonders how it happened, as it certainly not an easy career path, even in the UK where the field is much more developed than in other European countries. She did her PhD in Edinburgh and at the time was already involved in a lot of teaching and lecturing. After completion of her PhD, she lined up a number of internships, first at Science Media Center, where she worked on gathering science information for broadcasting. She then interned on science policy/science and society at the Department for  Business Innovation & Skills (BIS), and further moved to the Human Tissue Regulation office to work on ethics and regulation surrounding the use of cells and tissues. Her present job materialized after a last internship at Sense about Science ( http://www.senseaboutscience.org/ )  where she is now a Scientific Liaison officer. Tabitha points out that throughout this stretch of internships, she held neither a permanent not a paid/well-paid job  for an entire year! So be realistic about what comes your way if you try to move into science communication.

    Tabitha’s average day: no two days are similar! She’ll attend seminars, meetings, do administrative tasks, brief people, gather information from scientists – the only thing her job does not include is science education. Sense about Science mainly engages in issues that are already in the news, tries to help people to make sense of scientific issues, and to take their own informed decisions (for example, whether to vaccinate your children or whether to eat GM food). Sense about Science was created in 2007, partially out of frustration over how scientifically ignorantly the media handled the MMR vaccine issue. The events showed that there was a need for better information to reduce such misinformation of public opinion, to improve the use of scientific methods by the broad public, and to eradicate persistent misconceptions. Sense about Science does this by educating the public through recruitment of expert speakers, or public interventions like the “Ask for evidence“ campaign ( http://www.senseaboutscience.org/pages/a4e.html ).

     

    Tabitha’s advice to us: there is no need to box up science and communication separately, the two overlap in many areas and it is up to you how much of each you end up doing. It’s important throughout your career to hone both skill sets, and Tabitha’s surprised how much her PhD skills are valued in her present job; she’s convinced she couldn’t do it without her PhD. However, not all is fun; there are crazy deadlines and long hours to work, and some horrendously difficult tasks just as in lab research. A good opportunity is to stay in research and work in communication on the side, since many jobs aren’t well paid. Blogs, social media, events, festivals, campaigns, freelance science journalism, comedy, art, policy debates all offer many opportunities to get involved while still in lab.

     

    Questions for Tabitha:

     

    Q: Who pays for the information provided by Sense about Science?

    TI: We are a charity, so we’re constantly looking for funding, and about 30% is from public donations.

     

    Q: Who asks for information at Sense about Science?

    TI: Anyone can approach us, organizations, media, anyone with questions or needing advice.

     

    Q: What’s your next step with this kind of job, is there a long-term career plan?

    TI: Science communication is a very large field, there are many jobs out there but few are permanent, which means it’s very common to move around.

     

    Q: Do you need to be an expert in your field?

    TI: No, I’m not a specialist in almost all the things I’ve worked in. Sometimes being unfamiliar makes it more easy to communicate with other non-specialists.

     

    Q: How do you chose your experts for advice?

    TI: We have a large researchers’ database built over the years where we can pick our experts.

     

    Q: What does Voice of Young Science ( http://www.senseaboutscience.org/pages/voys.html ) do?

    TI: VoYS is an engaged group of early career researchers, they run campaigns, join forces to change things.

     

    Q: Is there any psychology involved in getting out the “right” messages to the public?

    TI: There is no PR for science, its’ more direct, we try to get the truth/information straight to where it’s needed.

     

    Your career blogger: Claudine Neyen
