    Careers in Pharma/Biotech Careers in Pharma/Biotech

    Career in Pharma/Biotech

    Tuesday 6th of May 2014

    Speakers: Jeff Behrens, Brian Hashemi and Prof. Christopher Tucci (MoT)

    Jeff Behrens, MS, MBA is an entrepreneur. He is president and CEO of Sialix, a company that develops antibody-based cancer therapeutics. He is based in Boston, USA and gives lectures about biomedical engineering at MIT.

    A former NASA Principal Investigator and Researcher Dr. Brian Hashemi is a Managing Partner at Salus Partners heading the healthcare investment and advisory services of the firm.  Brian has extensive background in healthcare technology development and commercialization and has been an Assistant Professor at Baylor College of Medicine where he began his affiliation in the National Space Biomedical Research Institute.  Recognizing the growing global demand for Health Technologies he has developed a strategic approach at Salus Partners for investments that accelerate expansion of promising proven medical and health technology innovations with global impact in healthcare.

     

    If there was any residual doubt as to the Life Sciences community’s interest in industry, management and business careers, this event was perfect proof:  Christopher    Tucci started the session to a full house. His opening message: a video broadcast from a fortunate academic giving advice on “career alternatives” with a twist (watch here). In this video, Gregory Petsko, professor at Weill Medical College, has a simple message: he and his academic colleagues ARE the alternative, the lucky few; most science graduates and postdocs go to the mainstream: industry.

    Jeff Behrens, who is currently doing a PhD with Tucci, opened the stage by asking us whether we wanted to work in Biopharma, or rather be Life Science Entrepreneurs. He promised to make sure we knew the difference in the end. After a quick poll in the room, about 20% still hopefully cling on to the academic ladder, another 15% want to start their own company, while the remaining majority is planning a move to the pharmaceutical industry.

    Jeff then gave a quick run-through of his career, before launching a recruitment pitch for his present company, Sialix. Jeff started out studying philosophy at Harvard, and was already running his own computer consulting company for undergrads and local labs before even finishing his first degree. His main funds came from selling a then rather rare service expensively to customers. What started as a side job turned into a proper business as The Telluride Group, which he sold to Mindshift in 2003. Advice he shares with us from this early experience: make sure your business partner is not also your partner in life.

    After the company was sold, Jeff wanted to do something in health care, but realized that his zero life science experience might be a bit of a hindrance. Since going back to school in his mid-thirties had little appeal, he first volunteered as a chemist in a lab and worked 6 months for free. During this time he came across the joint MSc and MBA program at Harvard/MIT, a 2-year course that combines Health Sciences and Technology. The ideal part in the program for Jeff was the internship included in the summer break between the 2 course years. He spent his summer at Biogen Idec, and talking with researchers made him aware of the need for a science/technology incubator for local biotech. He stayed on after the internship, designed a business plan for an incubator and helped building up several start-ups, in which he climbed as far as sub-CEO level before becoming CEO of Sialix, the company he works for now.

    Lessons from Jeff’s experience with biotech:

    #1           Big pharma needs constant innovation because income disappears once patents expire and drugs are replaced by generics.

    #2           Drug development for innovation, however is slow, hard, risky, costly. Big pharma doesn’t want to get in at the low end of development – they’d rather outsource innovation to small specialized biotech companies and buy up the successful drugs that come out at the end of the development pipeline. Also, “virtual” biotech is developing fast:  technical aspects of R&D are outsourced to specialized, globally distributed low-cost companies.

    #3 Entrepreneurs should try to enter the pipeline earlier on, where they do not compete with the big companies and retain the opportunity of selling to big pharma if the drug is successful. Their business model goes something like this:  raise money to spend on R&D, without ever expecting revenue or profit. The ultimate customer is big pharma who might buy the company if the product is successful.

    Jeff then pitched his own, academia-founded company Sialix to the audience to see who’d be willing to join. Enthusiastic applicants were met with 3 questions you should generally ask yourself before jumping head-first into industry: why would you work for this company? Is it a good idea at all? Why should you not work for a particular company? Motivations among “applicants” were a change in research topic, a salary increase compared to postdoc, but also apprehension at working for a large company with little exposure to different job aspects.

    Jeff’s list of things to consider includes: bench versus non-bench jobs: if you start as a bench scientist, you’ll be at the same level as PhDs, without much more seniority or pay. Bench roles typically do not involve managing a team, and the highest goal to aspire to will be CSO (Chief Scientific Officer). On the positive side, you’ll get to learn a broad spectrum of science.

    As opposed to academia, industry research is more team-oriented, and under more time pressure as deadlines matter economically. The focus shifts from publishing to moving the program forward, and a large part of work is just to reproduce, reproduce, reproduce results.

    The size of the company matters enormously, and will give you a completely different experience: in big biotechs, research will be more streamlined, there will be in-house training and mentoring, resources will be endless, with lots of toys and equipment to experiment on, and most importantly a brand name to add value to your CV. A drawback is the increase in bureaucracy in larger companies, which slows things down and makes it difficult to see the bigger picture. In small biotechs, on the contrary, it will be easier to grasp the full picture, and you’ll likely gain experience across the board of jobs. You’ll be closer to the boss, but will have no brand name attached to your job, and resources will be more limited. And if the company fails, you finish with unknown company that fail in your CV. As to job security, neither big nor small biotech is a guarantee for life-long employment…

    Non-bench roles in biotech include project management, quality assessment, consulting, technology licensing (which is often through partnerships with academia), intellectual property, medical affairs, or marketing strategies. These jobs are best accessed with a double PhD and MBA. So if you are looking into spending your total postdoc income on an MBA, make sure you assess the 6Cs: content (the least important because you can find it in books and online), credentials (the name matters enormously for MBAs), contacts (which are probably the most important reason for enrolling), career (aim for 2-year MBAs with an internship), cash (yes…surprise), and opportunity cost (what else could you be doing while on the MBA?).

    If you decide against the MBA, how do you get started in biotech?

    ·        Look out for postdoc positions in industry – they may be hard to come by, but will get you right in.

    ·        Do an internship, expect it to be unpaid.

    ·        Network intensely outside academia.

    ·        Find mentors, which is critical for your personal development – they should be people that you want to be some years down the line.

    ·        Try to do contract work or consulting.

    ·        Do random informational interviews – ask about people’s experience in the company to gauge whether you’d like their job.

    ·        Find out who YOU can help.

    ·        Keep going at it, the process doesn’t end with the 1st job you land.

     

     

     

     

    Brian Hashemi then took over and first traced his path from physics undergrad to Managing Partner at Salus Partners, a Swiss-based investment firm with special expertise in proven healthcare technologies.

    Brian’s core advice to us: Look out for those windows of opportunity, and go for them, because they’ll close right after you.

     

    As a kid, Brian always wanted to build rockets. Or that failing, to be a firefighter. But first, he met a fantastic mentor who encouraged him to study physics, so he embarked on a physics degree for which he ended up needing some biology. The only vegetarian-compatible, non-dissection class he could attend was cell biology, which got him hooked and into a PhD in cellular biophysics at Cornell University. Then his window of opportunity opened: NASA had just started exploring the medical impact of space flight on the immune system, and with his biophysics background, Brian fitted their bill. He worked for NASA and became an assistant professor at Baylor College of Medicine.

    So how exactly did he get from outer space to Lake Geneva? Well, second piece of advice from Brian: Don’t limit your options by thinking you need to follow a rigid path. Intelligence and enthusiasm for learning new things are attributes that certainly aren’t limited to academic careers.

    Brian traces his passion for health care back to his days as a firefighter (yes, he did get both!). During university, he was a first responder to emergency calls, which brought him face to face with traumatic events and forged his conviction that he should do something to help people. How to best approach this? Embark on another career change? Brian decided that he had one more in him, and with a friend set out to set up a health care company. With only research experience so far, he felt he needed some business schooling and did an executive MBA, then made a 5-year exit plan to leave NASA and start his journey to industry. A main difference according to Brian is time: academic research has the luxury of time – industry doesn’t.

    Looking at the WHO’s future trends in global mortality shows where to target research, innovation and investments: preventive medicine for diseases of an ageing population. Brian decided to focus on health care technology, ageing care and disease prevention. The EU is a hotspot for medical tech innovation, and Switzerland ranks highest in the EU, so Brian took a sabbatical from NASA, moved to Geneva and launched the first Med Tech Summit, a kind of technology Olympics. The Med Tech summit runs every two years in Switzerland, and brings together selected companies and investors to facilitate networking. For everyone attending, this is an excellent opportunity to see what’s going on in the sector. (Read about the last one here)

                  

    Questions to the speakers:

     Q?          Is it smart to join a (small) start-up?

    A:           You need to have an elevated tolerance rate to failure – if you can shrug off set-backs and don’t fear change, it’s a great opportunity to grow.

     Q? to JB:              What’s the exit strategy for pharma biotechs?

    JB:          Sell to a bigger pharma – there is no long-term aim for pharma start-ups. A sustainable model for start-ups is to get angels in to fund, develop a product, then sell.

    BH:         There are different models – a new development is for physicians to come with ideas and suggestions and to get entrepreneurs to develop solutions.

    JB:          The main issue with this model is that you need rich big pharma down the line to keep buying developed drugs. If highly priced drugs disappeared because of health policies, then the model would no longer work.

    Tucci:    Large companies strongly control markets and distribution, so yes, the only way things might change is by opening distribution to others, outside big pharma.

     Q:           Is the industry/academia split necessarily a forking path? Or can they be combined?

    JB:          The best academics are those that engage with industry and inform development. It is more likely though that industry engages with academics, not the other way round (see Nestlé).

     

      Your career blogger: Claudine Neyen
