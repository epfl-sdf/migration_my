Patrick Thiran, École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne

 

Inference for Networks and Networks for Inference

We all use networks (such as the Internet) and we are all parts of networks (such as the human social graph).

On the one hand, networks give us access to vast amounts of information and knowledge, but they are also the conduits for epidemics. Can we infer the source of a rumor or of a virus in a complex network? It is usually impossible to observe the state of all nodes in a network, so where would we put vaccination control or observation units, and how many? We show that it is fundamentally possible to estimate the location of the source of an epidemic from measurements collected by sparsely placed observers. In the context of several case studies, we determine how localization accuracy is affected by various system parameters. We also outline some possible strategies to contain the diffusion of a virus, once its source is located, by using minimal counter-measures.

In our interaction with networks on the other hand, we unwittingly reveal private information that can be used to infer knowledge about our whereabouts, preferences and activities. We illustrate this with two problems. The first one is the estimation of the size of a population or its density by using data collected from a few contact points. We conducted an experiment with participants who carry phones with active Bluetooth at the Paleo, an open-air music festival; we showed that this inference task is possible even with a handful of contact points. The second one is the quantitative evaluation of the predictability of our mobility trajectories, which is a first step for controlling the trade-offs between the utility provided by using location-based services offered by a network and the resulting potential loss of privacy.

Biography

Patrick Thiran is a full professor in network and systems theory at the School of Computer and Communication Sciences at EPFL. He holds an electrical engineering degree from the Université Catholique de Louvain, Louvain-la-Neuve, Belgium, an M.Sc. degree in electrical engineering from the University of California at Berkeley, USA, and he received the PhD degree from EPFL, in 1996. He became an adjunct professor in 1998, an assistant professor in 2002 and an associate professor in 2006. He was with Sprint Advanced Technology Labs in Burlingame, California, in 2000-01.

His research interests include communication networks, performance analysis, dynamical systems, and stochastic models. He is currently active in the analysis and design of wireless and PLC networks, in network monitoring, and in data-driven network science. He served as an Associate Editor for the IEEE Transactions on Circuits and Systems in 1997-99, and as an Associate Editor for the IEEE/ACM Transactions on Networking in 2006-10. He is/was on the program committee of different conferences in networking, including Sigcomm, Sigmetrics, IMC, CoNext and Infocom. He was the recipient of the 1996 EPFL Doctoral Prize and the 2008 Crédit Suisse Teaching Award.
