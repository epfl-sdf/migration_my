    Successfully managing a lab

    Successfully managing a lab

    NOTE TO READERS:

    Below are excerpts from the event, including speakers' presentations and the Q&A session. Although we try to take very quick and extensive notes for those of you who couldn't make it, the text is not always a verbatim quote. But we hope the main messages are there. 

    THANKS to the speakers for agreeing to make their combined brain-storming and advice available!

     

    Leadership Series

     

    How do I successfully manage a lab and staff?

     

    By - Prof. Richard Benton

    - Prof. Brenda Kwak

    We all have ideas about what (not) to do and what would be the ideal environment for ourselves. But, does that work for everyone? We have invited 2 academic group leaders that will share the pearls and pitfalls of leading their group with you. This will hopefully help you to further develop your own leadership style.

     

    Registered: 90 postdocs, 40 PhD students, 8 others

    Short biography of the speakers:

    Richard Benton received his PhD in 2003 from the University of Cambridge. He joined Leslie Vosshall's laboratory at The Rockefeller University, New York, as an EMBO/Heley Hay Whitney post-doctoral fellow. He established his group at the Center for Integrative Genomics in September 2007, and was awarded an ERC Starting Grant in 2008. He was promoted to Associate Professor in August 2012. In 2013, he was awarded an ERC Consolidator Grant.

    Brenda Kwak did her medical studies and her PhD in Amsterdam, with a short stint to the Albert Einstein College of Medicine in New York, followed by a postdoc in Utrecht. She then followed her partner to Switzerland and continued her line of research in the Department of Internal Medicine – Cardiology at the University of Geneva, first on a Marie-Heim-Vögtlin fellowship, then as SNF professor and since 2009 as an Associate professor. She has been a group leader since 2003 and teaches medical students at the Geneva faculty of medicine.

     

    Leadership styles are as varied as the leaders, but some essentials hold true across disciplines. Uri Alon, a leading figure in team management, motivation and the human complexities of science, states some of his convictions on how to build a motivated team in print. He also brought them forward during a fun TEDx talk last year in Lausanne. Since he wasn’t available live again, we watched his improvisation theatre take on scientific management to start off the career event.

     

    Then Richard Benton and Brenda Kwak took over with their combined vision on lab management, sharing slides, questions and comments as they went along.

    Their first question: if management is defined as dealing with or controlling people, then how does this translate to lab management in particular? How do you learn to do it, do you need training? Learn by experience?

    Coming from the fruit fly field, Richard Benton first presented two potential visions of labs, not the most ideal but likely frequent: the queen ant of a colony gorged by tireless insignificant workers, or a headless mingle of larvae eating out each other’s guts. Of course, you wouldn’t want to be part of either team. Richard’s idea of a research team is a combination of a small company or business, a group with common goals and a budget, but each individual also creatively pursuing their own projects.

    For Brenda Kwak, one crucial element in team management is communication – bringing together different people with different scientific backgrounds and motivations to tackle a complex problem. Diversity is what makes the richness of the team, making the diversity work as a whole is the team leader’s job.

    What does the team manager do? RB and BK put it as follows: try to get things done as clearly and in as structured a manner as possible (and accept the rest as it is).

    RB admits that in order to run a successful lab, you have to step back and stop doing the research yourself. BK agrees – the moments she gets to do a tiny bit of experiment are scarcely spread. But she compensates with what both team leaders deem essential: staying close and up-to-date with their team’s primary data.

    So what challenges are in the “team management” package? Trying to manage people, projects, collaborations, finances, administration, teaching, ethics, safety, … a full slide of topics (reprinted below) that RB and BK then encouraged the audience to quiz them about.

     

    People

    ·         How to hire great people?

    ·         How to motivate people?

    ·         How to let people go?

    ·         How to deal with conflicts?

    ·         How big should a lab be?

    ·         How to delegate?

    Projects

    ·         How to decide who does what project?

    ·         How do you balance core lab interests and risky/crazy things?

    ·         How to manage project progress?

    ·         How to balance PI vs trainees’ careers?

    ·         How to agree on what projects stay or leave the lab?

    ·         How to maintain lab legacy/memory?

    Publishing

    ·         When and where to publish?

    ·         How do you decide who gets authorship?

    ·         How do you determine order of authors?

    ·         Conflict of interest for reviewing?

    Collaborators (external)

    ·         How to find collaborators?

    ·         How to work with collaborators?

    ·         How to manage consortia?

    ·         How to deal with industry?

    Finances

    ·         How to make a budget?

    ·         Is the question “worth” the budget?

    ·         Financial accountability?

    Administration

    ·         How to deal with email?

    ·         How to work with a secretary?

    ·         How to function in commissions?

    Ethics and safety

    ·         How to avoid/deal with misconduct?

    ·         How to handle lab safety?

    Teaching

    ·         Continuous changes/improvement?

    ·         Do objectives match federal requirements?

                           

     Q?          How do you know how to hire the right people?

    RB: This is the biggest challenge. To hire a postdoc you look for someone who has proven ability to take a project from A to Z, so you look first at their publication record. Then recommendation letters: you should definitely speak to the referee directly, because letters need reading between the lines. This flags mismatches between what is stated in a letter and what the referee really says. Then, you need visibility to attract people, so do speak at conferences, meetings etc.  Qualities you look for in potential collaborators: people you can get on with is very important – if you can’t have an easy chat with someone, don’t hire them. Also, as a young PI with lower visibility, be pro-active about hiring, ask colleagues in the field to pass around advertisements or recommend people.

    BK: If you have any doubt at all, don’t hire. You need to meet the person and feel comfortable with them. Usually I use my networks to recruit, but have also advertised positions. Frequently candidates are too far away to just invite them for a chat, so I have a skype conversation with them before, and then select a few to invite. I have everyone in the lab talk to them and take them for a beer…Then I get feedback on how the team feels about the candidate, which is a safe tell.

    What is more difficult though than hiring is letting people go: not only because you need to acknowledge you made a mistake in the first place, but it’s hard to speak to the person and be diplomatic.

    RB: Letting go is easier if you follow the classic management strategies/techniques: set the person goals, do regular assessments and if they fail to meet your expectations it’s a clear-cut decision why they’re being let go.

     

    Q? What is the first person we should hire when starting a lab?

    RB: The first person I hired was a technician; in Switzerland these are trained personnel. Hiring junior or short-term staff (for example, at the Master level) can be an excellent strategy because their tenure in you lab is like a long interview: you get to know the person’s abilities and you can assess their suitability for a PhD.

    BK: My first hire was a postdoc from my network, someone I had known personally for a while so it was a safe guess. I agree with Richard that safe hires are Master students that you can assess for their PhD suitability.

     

    Q? Mentoring: why aren’t there any psychologists involved in recruiting leaders in academia, as there are in industry – wouldn’t it make PhD students’ and postdoc’s experience better if they had great mentors?

    RB: Academia is still quite unstructured when it comes to training and assessing mentors. However, experience is a very important way of learning how to be a good supervisor. You should try to mentor as many people as possible during your postdoc – undergraduates, summer students, high school students, technicians - you learn by trial-and-error how to manage and motivate people.

    BK: Managing people needs continuous adaptation; you learn to manage everyone according to their needs. Managing is based on interaction, on feedback and resetting and adaptation. That said, in Geneva the assistant professors have a mentoring scheme in place for them. This isn’t the case in all institutions, so a word of advice: find a mentor who is just a step ahead of you, who’s freshly gone through the steps you are about to take, and ideally someone from the same institution who knows how things work.

     

    RB: Another question linked to mentoring is how to motivate people to their best abilities?

    Motivation, for scientists, is largely self-generated, and almost impossible to force. It doesn’t work to ask your team, “why aren’t you as passionate about this research as I am”? The best way is to lead by example: be endlessly enthusiastic about your work, always keep a positive outlook, bounce back from the everyday misery of failing experiments and rejected publications, pick people up rather than pushing them down.

    BK: For this reason it’s important that you chose your topic well; it needs to be something that you are really passionate about, in order to be able to motivate others.

     

    Q? How do you decide who does what? Do you assign people to core lab projects or do you let them do crazy/risky things?

    BK: You decide or at least discuss at the moment of hiring what a person’s project is going to be. And you should definitely let them do something that they are passionate about, and technically attuned too;  for example, some people have great hands for molecular biology, for others it’s a struggle. For post-docs it’s important that they can develop something that they can use as the basis for their own research niche for when they leave your lab to start an independent career.

    RB: At the start, when you build a lab, you have your own projects that you want done, for funding, a kind of 5-year plan. This doesn’t fit with hiring creative individuals who will develop their own project, so with the first people you hire, you should be honest: give them the 5-year plan outline, but encourage them to explore something else as well.

     

    Q? How to delegate?

    RB: If you have technicians in your team, you can delegate more tasks to help in the smooth running of the lab, and you can train them in things you’ll have no time to do yourself. Postdocs and PhD students in my lab also have a list of shared, recurrent tasks to do and we have lab cleanings once or twice a year, so there is also a sense of community involvement in lab maintenance.

    BK: Delegating the research is the hard thing to learn when you are just starting your own research group; for example, you’d like to keep doing experiments but there is little time.

     

    Q? How do you manage finances, how do you chose which starting grants to apply for?

    BK: These are things that you can learn from a mentor within your institute. You need to strike a balance: be realistic about your budget (factor in salaries, bench fees, overheads for the institute), and ask yourself whether your research is worth the budget (are you doing a random fishing expedition or do you have clear-cut goals, for example). Also learn to work with foundations – what do they expect in return? Project summaries? Feedback?

    RB: Grants are given to you for your excellent previous research and your new ideas put forward in grant applications. But often science takes you on other paths, so it’s difficult to stay exactly within the grant content. It’s OK as long as you stay in the general field; there is no need to justify point by point what you’ve been doing with the grant money. In any case, if you’re doing exactly what you said you’d do in your grant several years on, then you have to ask whether the original proposal was too predictable! But still, don’t waste money.

    BK: For long-term grants, there is no need to stick exactly to the described experiments, but you need to answer the research question.

     

    Q? How to avoid scientific misconduct?

    RB: By not putting excessive pressure on your team and by staying in touch with the primary data. You need to spot if things aren’t well done early on.

    BK: I agree, it is extremely important to see the original data.

     

    In summary, good lab management is based on the following:

    1)      Honest but diplomatic communication

    2)      Sensitivity to everyone’s needs

    3)      Be well organized but don’t micromanage

    4)      Know how to say no (but getting involved in reviewing papers/grants, organising meetings etc is a great way to build a network and is part of your duties as lab head)

    5)      Rely on a large network (scientific and non-scientific)

    6)      Have lots of positive energy

    7)      Constant adaptation: don’t be stuck in a style

     

    Books to read: Richard recommends “At the Helm” by Kathy Barker. Look out for courses (EMBO runs a great lab management course), but it may be more useful to take one of these only after you have been running your lab for a little while. And of course it helps to discuss your issues with your own mentor and other PIs, or people outside science, who can give an external perspective.

     

    If you want to read more on this topic, also have a look at our Career event: Starting your own lab (2013).

    Another recommended resource is "Making the Right Moves", a compendium of advice for the starting young PI, freely available on the web.

     

    Your career blogger: Claudine Neyen
