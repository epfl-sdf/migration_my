    How the Vanadium Redox Flow Battery (VRB) works

     

    Published on 25 Jun 2012
    HOW THE VANADIUM REDOX BATTERY (VRFB) WORKS

    MARIA SKYLLAS-KAZACOS
    Professor Emeritus, University of New South Wales and Advisory Board Member of PacificOre Mining Corp.

    Maria is pioneering the next generation of VRFB batteries PacificOre Mining Corp. owns two of the largest Vanadium deposits in the world and the largest Vanadium deposits in America capable of supplying the large quantities of "battery grade" Vanadium Pentoxide required for VRFBs

     

    Redox flow batteries - an overview

     

    Introduction
    Redox flow batteries (RFBs) are electrochemical energy storage systems for medium to large-scale applications. They are one of the few technologies capable of storing intermittent electrical energy and deliver electrical energy on demand (once charged). The technology is therefore a major player in the energy future of the planet, as we strive to decrease our dependence on fossil fuels and increasingly integrate renewable energy sources. A number of very good scientific reviews are available to fully discuss RFB technology (1-3). Below is a brief overview for the casual reader, however.
     
    The concept
    Redox flow batteries are rechargeable electrochemical systems. They differ to conventional batteries as they do not contain solid materials, but dissolved ionic species capable of taking on and releasing electrons as desired. Solutions of concentrated ions, which have a specific energy are flowed from two separate storage tanks into an electrochemical cell. The cell comprises two electrodes and a membrane separating the two liquid flows but allowing the transport of protons from one side to the other.  This is important as when an electron (negative charge) moves from one half of the battery to the other, and equal positive charge (proton) must also move the same direction to maintain electroneutrality. When charging a voltage is applied to the cell such that the negative electrode delivers electrons to the negative solution (catholyte) and extracts electrons from the positive solution (anolyte). The solutions pass back into their respective storage tanks, and remain in a charged state until needed. To discharge the battery a load is attached and the charged solutions pass through the cell once again. This time the negative solution returns the electrons it received in charging, and the positive solution accepts them, bringing both solutions back to their original state.     The voltage delivered by the battery depends on the ions used. The catholyte has a negative redox potential relative to the anolyte. The difference between their redox potentials (i.e. the energy required to reduce the ion) indicates the maximum voltage difference the battery can deliver. For example, if the catholyte is reduced (or oxidised) at -0.2 V and the charged anolyte is reduced at +0.5 V then the battery can have a voltage of +0.7 V. Careful choice of redox ions is therefore necessary to get a productive battery, and so a large variety has been studied.
     
     All-vanadium RFBs
    The all-vanadium redox flow battery (VRB) is the most well-known flow battery system, and the most successfully commercialised flow battery to-date. The key to the system is multivalent vanadium. Vanadium has 4 stable oxidation states in aqueous solutions; these are summarised in the table below along with their formal reduction potential in a pH 1 solution.
     
     
    The VRB uses the 3+/2+ couple in the catholyte and the 5+/4+ couple in the anolyte so the potential difference, or voltage, obtained from the battery is 1.26 V (i.e. 1.00V -(-0.26 V)). The battery has the advantage that cross contamination of ions through the membrane central to the battery cells is less detrimental than in mixed species battery, as other than self discharge, contamination of the electrolytes is avoided. The potentials are also such that water oxidation to oxygen is avoided at the anode (positive electrode during charging) and hydrogen evolution is negligible at the cathode (negative electrode during charging).     Advantages and Disadvantages of RFBs
    Advantages:
    ·         Power and capacity are separated - Power scales with number of electrochemical cells whereas capacity scales with volume of electrolyte
    ·         Modular system - adapts to the users needs
    ·         Rapid response from charge to discharge
    ·         Long cycle lifetime - RFBs can operate with minimal maintenance for over 10 years
    ·         Non-toxic components
    ·         Non-precious components - lowers overall cost
    Disadvantages:
    ·         Low energy density per metre squared
    ·         Large installations required
    ·         High initial costs
    ·         Limited to all-vanadium systems (presently) due to ion-crossover through the membrane
    ·         Cannot be charged/discharged quickly
