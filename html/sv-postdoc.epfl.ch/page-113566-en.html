    Leadership in Science

       Leadership in Science Event

      18th September 2014

     

    Organisers: Verena Pfeiffer and Paulina Wachowicz

    Speakers: Sašo Kočevar, HFP Consulting & Professor Gisou Van der Goot, Dean of SV

     

    Sašo Kočevar

     

    HFP consulting deals with the practical side of leadership. There are eighteen consultants, the majority of whom are scientists. HFP runs courses on leadership for may different customers including EMBO, Universities across Poland, BBS graduate programme at MIT, and the Bill Gates Foundation, to name a few.

     

    What’s all the hype about leadership?!

    We used to view science as a man sitting on his own in a room working in an objective and rational way. Now our vision of science is changing. People realise that science is a human endeavour that involves collaboration, and that humans are subjective and emotional.

     

    Turn to your neighbour and tell them why you are in science! (and everyone did, apparently the room was then filled with smiles!)

    Fulfilment and achievement leads to happiness.

     

    Why do we need leaders?

    ·       To help us use the positive aspects that individuals bring to collaboration and to help us live with the negative aspects that people can bring. To take the lead to avoid conflicts of interest causing problems.

    ·       To encourage and to help people live to their full potential.

     

    Remember that you can lead without being in charge!

     

    In science leadership is not taught - it’s rather a case of sink or swim! In industry, on the other hand, new recruits are sent off for weeks of workshops and training courses on management and leadership. It is important!

     

    Leadership Styles

    ·       There isn’t only one!

    ·       Situation dependent (encouragement vs. crisis situation, for example)

     

    What skills do you need to be a good leader?

    • ·       Social interaction
    • ·       Guidance
    • ·       Listening
    • ·       Empathy
    • ·       Enthusiasm
    • ·       The ability to play a role - be enthusiastic even if you might be having a tough day yourself - it will also make you feel better!
    • ·       Stability - very important in times of crisis and conflicts.
    • ·       Rituals (celebrate papers being accepted, or even submitted)

     

    Tools

    • ·       Active listening
    • ·       Delegation
    • ·       Language
    • ·       Negotiation

     

    You can learn all this! You don’t have to be a natural born leader - everyone can learn how to lead. All personality types can be good leaders. All you need is awareness and getting hold of the right tools, skills and experience. In time, it will become an attitude.

     

    HFP run workshops in leadership and management in science, for all stages of the scientific career. Sašo highly recommends the “female leaders in science” workshop, which was initiated in 2013.

     

    Questions 

     

    Q. Do you really think that more experienced people are better leaders, or do people become more disconnected with their staff/postdocs as time goes on?

    A. Both. You will always see things differently as you move along in your career. When you are a young PI you are not a postdoc anymore and things will appear differently to you.

     

    Q. Can a postdoc change leadership around them?

    A. You can take the lead and express your needs. You can’t address other people’s problems.

     

    Q. Are there some tools to use when managing men vs. women?

    A. Think about how you want feedback and criticism to be given in your group. How do we want to discuss this project? How do we want to interact with each other?

     

    Q. Can you do it all: be human, play a role, stay positive, be genuine?

    A. Yes. It’s a tricky balance, but you can do it. The important thing is to stay genuine.

     

    Q. How do you lead in a team in which you are not in charge?

    A. Framesetting: manage your meetings! What do you want to achieve in a given session? What’s the goal, who has which responsibility?

     

    Gisou

     

    Recommends going on courses. There are tricks that you can learn!

     

    What does leadership mean to you?

    • ·       There seems to be a misconception in science that leadership is scientific vision. Interestingly, Sašo didn’t mention vision.
    • ·       Being a leader is being the driving force - being the person that does the things that need to be done to make things happen! If you are the motor driving a project, then you are a leader, whether it’s in a scientific collaboration, in the Postdoc Association, organising lab apéros, whatever.
    • ·       Seize opportunities and take risks!
    • ·       It’s like being a skipper on a boat. Everyone on the boat has a crucial role, but in a crisis you need one person to make a decision - the leader.
    • ·       Addressing conflicts - the sooner the better.
    • ·       Delegation - you need to trust people in your team. Being a perfectionist is a disaster!
    • ·       Feedback is extremely important. Ask for it.
    • ·       Listening
