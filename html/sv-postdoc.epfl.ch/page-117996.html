    Communicating Science to the public

    Career Event

    15.01.2015: Communicating Science to the public

     

    By Prof. Daniel Cherix and Vivienne Baillie Gerritsen

    • Vivienne Baillie Gerritsen is a popular science writer who devotes half her time to writing the beautifully crafted protein snapshots for the Swiss Institute of Bioinformatics (have a look here) and the other half to projects that revolve around words. She runs her own publishing house where you can track some of her works.
    •  Prof. Daniel Cherix is Honorary Professor of the Department of Ecology and Evolution at UNIL and honorary conservationist at the Museum of Zoology in Lausanne. His passion for communicating science got him several radio shows and quite a few stories to tell.

     

    Vivienne and Daniel will discuss how to communicate science to the public, also touching on Science & Society topics.

     Thursday 15 January 2015

     Attendance: 30 postdocs, 18 PhD students, 10 other

     Below you’ll find an approximate transcript of the event.

     -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Quite tellingly for a communication event, both speakers brought no slides but plenty of entertaining stories, and kept their audience captivated throughout.

     

    VBG started by tracing what she called her rather “chaotic academic career”: an initial interest in anthropology and human evolution got her studying geology and carbon dating, since genetics of ancient hominids wasn’t very developed at the time. Two years in she got “fed up with stones” and moved to Geneva University to study archaeology, only to drop this again after two years. She then spent two years teaching maths and biology in primary schools, and eventually returned to do an undergraduate degree in Biology. As there was no space for a PhD student in the lab of her choice, she launched into science journalism for two local newspapers and realized just how much she enjoyed communicating science. During a period of odd jobs she was approached by SwissProt to start an online magazine called “Protein Spotlight”, and has been working here ever since. Run by the Swiss Institute of Bioinformatics (SIB), this initiative aims at bringing the beauty of protein structure to the broad public. Along with her day job as a science communicator, Vivienne has written books, in collaboration with art students, and has set up plays that ran at science fairs.

    She deplores that while 20 years ago there were no degrees on science communication, there still aren’t many nowadays, and science communication is mainly used as a tool to promote institutions.

    Her advice to us: get to know the field you want to work in – be it writing, telling stories, making movies, publishing books or writing journalistic articles.

      

    Questions to Vivienne:

     

    Q? How did you get into plays?

    VBG: Through my love of words – I love to play with words.

     Q? What kind of audience is reading your snippets on Protein Spotlight?

    VBG: I was asked to write for scientists but to make it fun. The audience are biologists and people aware of biology.

     Q? Did you develop a good method for storytelling over the years?

    VBG: I rely on the choice of words and try to make it human. If a person knows how to talk, they can get people interested in anything.

     Q? How do you construct a good storyline?

    VBG: I have no plan, it depends on the story and on the audience and then things happen. Artistic expression can guide the story – try to draw it before you write it. A general guideline would be, first know your audience, second know what you want to talk about and third know the message.

     Q? What are the outlets for the average scientist who wants to get involved in science communication? Where and how do you go about it?

    VBG: Switzerland is small, there aren’t many opportunities. The UK has a much better developed popular science landscape, with museums, science fairs and the like. For my book for example, it was my own initiative and I had to find the funding. In Switzerland science promotion is mostly institute-based, there are no overarching initiatives.

     Comments from the audience on how to get involved:

    Amanda Baker, who runs the Frontiers for Young Minds initiative at Frontiers, draws our attention to this very fun way of getting involved in communicating your favourite science to kids: scientists rewrite popular papers in 2000 words and submit them to young reviewers 8-15 years old to check for clarity (and these are very harsh reviewers with exacting standards!). 

    Q? Do you ever get criticism / critical feedback from scientists on your protein snippets? Do they validate what you write, or check the accuracy?

    VBG: Never. Occasionally I’ve received positive feedback.  But my texts are proofread for language and content.

     Q? Who do you think should write popular science content, the scientists themselves or journalists?

    VBG: The best would be for scientists to write about their own science, and for scientists to be trained in writing well.

     Comments from the audience on becoming a science journalist:

    A member from the audience who writes for Le Temps added that there are two ways to becoming a science journalist: one is to train at a journal after taking a science degree, which involves 9 weeks of courses alongside daily journalistic work, and delivers a journalist diploma at the end. The other is to attend a Masters course in journalism (e.g. at the University of Neuchâtel), which is a more academic path.

     Q? Where do you find funding for your books or plays?

    VBG: There are numerous places where you can apply for science communication funding, for example the Leenards Foundation or the Lotterie Romande, museums, the SNF…

     ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

     Prof Daniel Cherix then took over and traced his 40+ years in science, from admiring butterflies as a boy to running children’s activities in Lausanne’s Museum of Zoology. DC did his whole university education in Lausanne, ending with a PhD in entomology at UNIL. He then left Switzerland for a couple of postdoc years in the US and in Japan (where communicating science was somewhat hampered by the language barrier…) and eventually came back to Lausanne to start his parallel career at the museum and at the university.

     While doing his PhD on ants and talking about it to people, he very quickly realized the importance of finding common ground when talking about science: everybody knows ants and has a story about ants, and this will create starting points for discussion. During his postdoc in the US, where he worked on fire ants, he realized that the public wanted to know about his research because they had specific issues related to these ants (pests causing damage to farmers for example). Communicating research to lay people needs a different level of understanding than talking to rotation students in the lab, so DC learned to tell the same story made up of different words, concepts and imagery. To be understood, he says to keep it simple and concise.

    After his postdocs, DC applied to a job posting at the Museum of Lausanne who was looking for an animator. Museums live a double life, with on one hand the scientists producing knowledge, and on the other translating this knowledge to a very broad public (children, adults, scientists and lay people) demanding to be entertained. In DC’s experience, the best public to test something on are children (we’re strictly talking about science communication here!) – if they don’t get it, adults won’t get it either.

     In his job as an animator, each week meant creating new animations and movies on animals for kids. DC quickly found out that some bits of information were more memorable than others to kids, and learned to adapt his animations so to attract and maintain interest. For example, kids would not remember the complicated Latin name of a beast but remember perfectly well certain animal behaviours.

    DC then got asked by the “Service d’assistance scolaire”, an educational question time for kids on the radio, to answer questions about the animal world over the phone. While the show was initially recorded and the questions came with a bit of a warning, it later turned live and DC admits that without books nor time to think about questions, the best answer if you don’t know is simply to state that you don’t know. He cautions against making answers up as this reflects poor scientific practice. Either find something in the question that you are able to answer, and expand on that, speaking on your subject where you are the expert, or refer to a colleague you know to be an expert on the question. This goes for journalists too: if you don’t know, refer to specialists and don’t try to make up an answer on your own. Remember your circle of acquaintances and approach them for advice. Journalists usually need information immediately and accurately – you sharing your network with them is as important as knowing the answer yourself.

    Another piece of advice: you usually don’t have time to explain 100 years of previous research; journalists are more likely to give you 3 minutes so if you are using complex concepts you won’t have time to explain. Therefore speak about simple things that are easily explained and understood.

    DC also started writing a column for the Sunday newspaper Le Matin. The column presented animals, and made him aware of how to construct a story while keeping to essentials. DC’s advice: keep a red line running through your stories, to involve the public and keep them interested. Things to avoid at all cost are misinformation and misquoting, so be careful in checking the accuracy of your sources.

    In summary, if you want to get involved with popular science, test your skills on kids (a good starting point is L’Éprouvette, Lausanne University’s lab for the public). And expect teenagers to be your worst audience (try ugly gory stories, they might listen up).

     

     Questions to Daniel:

     Q? Would you reply “I don’t know” to a question?

    DC: Now yes, I would. But as a young person you might not want to do that, to keep your profile…See above, refer to an expert.

     Q? What about body language – did you learn this?

    DC: On the radio, nobody can see you; it’s all about what you say and your tone of voice. But they train you, and you learn by doing.

    Q? Please tell us about your daily life at the Museum.

    DC: It’s hectic. You still need to do research (and I like doing research), but you also need to prepare exhibitions and answer questions that anyone can send in.

     Q? How much freedom do you have in organizing exhibitions?

    DC: I’m usually quite free; I’ve rarely met with opposition.

     Q? How do you move between different publics, kids, adults?

    DC: To say it rudely, you simply use the same language… Use images to explain, stay short to focus people/avoid losing people. Adults are usually too polite to say they didn’t catch you. Kids are more open, they just ask.

     Q? How do you decide on the texts to use in exhibitions?

    DC: Textual information is difficult; you need to include multiple levels of information in an exhibition, to target different audiences. In general though, there has been a tendency towards using less text in museum exhibits, and towards conceptualization of ideas.

     Q? Is there a legal entity that controls the quality of science written by journalists? (referring to science snippets in the 20minutes for example)

    DC: The Department of Education and the Museum check our texts before we present them. But that’s a special case; journalists often don’t have time to fact check, so it remains the scientists’ responsibility to deliver accurate content. There is no control against bulls*, people or your colleagues trust you, so you have a moral obligation to be accurate.

     --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

     The SV postdoc association warmly thanks both speakers for taking the time to share their experience, advice and fun stories with us.

     (reported by Claudine Neyen)
